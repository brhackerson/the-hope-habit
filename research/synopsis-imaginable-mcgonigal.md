# Synopsis: *Imaginable*
**Jane McGonigal | Spiegel & Grau, 2022**
*Prepared for: Hope is Not a Feeling — research and differentiation notes*

---

## What the Book Is

*Imaginable* is a future-forecasting and imagination-training book by Jane McGonigal — a game designer, futurist, and NYT bestselling author who directs games research at the Institute for the Future and teaches "How to Think Like a Futurist" at Stanford. The book grew directly out of her experience designing a pandemic simulation game in 2010 that predicted — in remarkable detail — what COVID-19 would look like a decade later. Her argument: if we had practiced imagining that future, we would have been far less blindsided by it.

The book is structured in three parts and runs about 530 pages (including 50+ pages of references).

---

## The Three Parts

**Part One: Unstick Your Mind**
McGonigal makes the case for future thinking as a trainable skill. The central exercise is the "ten-year trip" — imagining your life in vivid, specific detail ten years from now. Ten years is far enough that significant change is likely, but close enough to feel real. She draws on neuroscience to explain why this is hard: MRI studies show that when you imagine your future self, your brain treats that person as a stranger — someone you have no emotional connection to. The practice of vivid future simulation builds that connection, making your future self feel real and worth investing in today.

**Part Two: Think the Unthinkable**
McGonigal introduces tools for spotting signals of change — early indicators of how the future might differ from the present. Key concepts here include "flipped facts" (imagining a future where the opposite of today's reality is true) and "future forces" (trends powerful enough to reshape society). The goal is developing cognitive flexibility — the ability to hold multiple possible futures in mind without being destabilized by uncertainty.

**Part Three: Imagine the Unimaginable**
The final section invites readers into three large-scale future simulation games McGonigal has designed. These are immersive thought experiments about global challenges — pandemics, climate, social disruption — where readers practice what they would do, feel, and decide if those futures arrived. The emotional rehearsal is the point: pre-feeling a future reduces anxiety and builds resilience when that future (or something like it) actually comes.

---

## Key Concepts

**Episodic Future Thinking (EFT)** — The psychological practice of mentally simulating specific future experiences in vivid detail. Research shows EFT reduces impulsive decision-making, increases long-term planning, and builds emotional connection to your future self. This is the scientific engine behind the whole book.

**Urgent Optimism** — McGonigal's term for the activated mindset that says: I see the challenges ahead clearly, and I believe I have the agency to respond to them. Not naive optimism. Not despair. A motivated, clear-eyed readiness to act. She describes it as "an unstoppable force within each of us that activates our sense of agency."

**Mental Time Travel** — The practice of imaginatively placing yourself in a specific future moment and rehearsing your emotional and practical responses. You pre-process anxiety before the crisis arrives, so you can move more quickly to adapt when it does.

**Signals of Change** — Early, often overlooked indicators that a significant shift is underway. McGonigal teaches readers to scan for these and use them as the raw material for future scenarios.

**Future Self as Stranger** — The neuroscience finding that drives the whole book. Because our brains treat our future selves as strangers, we consistently underinvest in them. Vividly imagining the future closes that gap — your future self becomes someone you care about and make decisions for today.

---

## What McGonigal Does Well

The book is thoroughly researched, practically structured, and genuinely useful for anyone who wants to feel less anxious about an uncertain future. The game-design background shows — the exercises are well-constructed and the scenarios are genuinely engaging. Daniel Pink called it "like sitting down with a creative, optimistic friend — and getting up as a new version of yourself."

Honest critique from readers: at 530 pages it runs long, some chapters repeat themselves, and the disaster scenarios in Part Three can feel heavy. The book is probably most useful read selectively rather than cover to cover.

## Relevance to *Hope is Not a Feeling*

This is the closest book in the competitive landscape and worth taking seriously. The overlap is real:

- Both argue that hope is actively created, not passively received
- Both are grounded in neuroscience research
- Both give readers practical tools rather than inspiration alone
- Both address the question of how to act when the future feels uncertain

**The key differentiation:**

McGonigal's tool is **future simulation** — imagining scenarios ten years out to build resilience and reduce anxiety. Her audience is people trying to navigate a rapidly changing world.

Brian's tool is **daily practice in the present moment** — showing up today, and again tomorrow, with self-discipline in the face of adversity. His audience is people who have lost hope in their own personal journey.

McGonigal looks forward ten years. Brian focuses on tomorrow morning.

One additional connection worth noting: "Pictures" — Brian's keyword — is essentially a personal future simulation. It places Brian in a specific, vivid future moment (the photographs, the grandchildren, the legacy) and uses that emotional connection to activate agency in the present. McGonigal's neuroscience explains exactly why this works. The Episodic Future Thinking research is directly applicable to the keyword mechanism and should be referenced when explaining why the keyword works in Chapter 6.

**Bottom line:** These books are not competing. They are in conversation. A reader who finds McGonigal useful might find Brian's book the practical next step — here's how to use that future vision to fuel a daily hope practice. Consider referencing *Imaginable* and the EFT research in Chapter 6 when explaining the neuroscience behind the keyword.
