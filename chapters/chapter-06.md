# Chapter 6: The Why Big Enough to Carry You

*Draft: July 2026*

---

Chapter 4 named the word. This chapter opens what's inside it.

Pictures arrived in early 2025 as a quick interruption tool, a first line of defense against a loud inner critic on bad mornings. That was enough for a while. One word, accessible in under a second, cutting through the noise before the decision to stay in bed became permanent. The word worked. What I didn't understand yet was what the word actually contained, or how much it would grow.

This chapter is about what it became, and why a keyword that works has to grow with the practice or it eventually stops working.

---

## Goals Fade. Causes Don't.

Simon Sinek's concept of the just cause names something most goal-setting frameworks miss.[⁸] A goal is finite. You reach it, or you don't, and either way the energy behind it dissipates. A just cause is different. It is a purpose worth the daily sacrifice, something larger than any single outcome, something you can lose a round for and still wake up wanting to play the next one.

The distinction matters for a keyword because most keywords start as goals. Lose this much weight. Hit this number. Reach this milestone. Goals are useful. They are not causes. A goal that gets met leaves you staring at the ceiling wondering why you don't feel the way you expected to feel. A cause that gets partially met leaves you wanting more of it.

Pictures qualifies as a just cause. Not because it describes a single future moment, but because it contains an entire architecture of people, occasions, and versions of presence that don't exist yet. I am still building every one of those moments. The keyword doesn't point at a finish line. It points at a reason to keep playing.

The failure mode is worth naming because it is so common. Someone starts a health journey with a goal: lose twenty pounds, run a 5K, get off the medication. They work hard. They hit the goal, or they come close. Then the energy drains. They can't explain why. Motivation carried them for months, and now it has gone quiet. They conclude that willpower failed them, or that they are the kind of person who starts things but doesn't finish them.

They are not. They had a goal, and goals exhaust themselves. The keyword was pointing at a finish line rather than a reason. The fix is not more discipline. It is a better word, pointing at something that doesn't end when the number on the scale changes.

That is what makes it survive the bad morning. A goal says: you should go to the gym because you have a target to hit. A just cause says: you should go to the gym because the person in those photographs is worth building. Those are different conversations, and only one of them wins when the inner critic is at full volume at 5 am.

## The Photographs That Don't Exist Yet

When I say the word and let it do what it normally does, the first thing that appears is not an abstract concept. It is a specific scene.

Emily's wedding comes first, though nothing is concrete yet. In the picture I am standing next to Debby. I am in a tux. She is in a beautiful dress. We look fabulous. We are probably about the same size we were when we married in 1989.

That detail matters more than it appears. The picture is not only a future anticipated. It loops back and completes something from the past. Standing there, the size we were when we started this life together, at our daughter's wedding decades later: that is the keyword doing its deepest work. It is pointing forward and backward at the same time. The emotion in the frame, before the frame exists, is pure joy.

That is what a just cause looks like when it becomes personal. Not a category like "family milestones." A literal scene, with literal people, in literal clothes, on a day that does not exist yet but feels close enough to reach.

The word contains others, and some of them surprised me the first time I let myself sit with them. Crossing the finish line of a 5K, upright and strong, not gutting it out. A round of golf at a course that has nothing to do with convenience or the calendar: Pebble Beach, Torrey Pines, a bucket-list layout I have watched on television for thirty years and never walked. Debby and I, retired, somewhere in Scandinavia or Italy, no itinerary that answers to anyone's schedule but ours. None of these are urgent. None of them are required for the practice to work today. That is precisely why they matter. A just cause does not need to be solemn to be real. It is a lot of fun to picture these things, and the fun itself is part of why they're worth building.

Future grandchildren belong in the word too, though they don't exist yet either. When they arrive, I want to be physically capable of meeting them wherever they'll want to play. Throwing a ball. Dribbling a soccer ball. Shooting a basketball. These are not abstract hopes. They are specific, physical, ordinary moments that require a body capable of doing them, not just a body that shows up to watch. A grandparent who can still get down on the ground to play, still chase a ball, still keep up, offers something different than a grandparent who can only watch from a chair. Both are presence. Only one is the full version I am building.

Minnesota winter does something to a body that takes a while to name. The shoulders rise over months without announcing it. The jaw tightens. The whole system contracts against the cold. On a trip south not long ago, we stepped off the plane and warm humid air arrived. Something released that the body had carried for months. Debby noticed it at the same moment I did. We looked at each other without needing to say much. Shoulders down.

That physical release was itself a receipt. The hope practice had built a body capable of noticing it, and a mind present enough to receive it. Planning more of those seasons, designing them in advance rather than waiting to see if they happen, is the practice applied to recovery. The specific future of winters in Florida is already shaping choices made today, and it doesn't stop at Florida. The same picture that gets us south for a season is the picture that gets us to Rome or a fjord in Norway a decade from now, still capable of walking all day on cobblestones or a gangway, still choosing the season instead of enduring it.

Winters in Florida with Debby, starting soon. Ordinary selfies. Different activities. A season of life we have never had together, because Minnesota winter has always been in the way. That is a near-term future, not a distant one. It is close enough to plan for, which is exactly why it works as a scene inside the keyword. The more specific a future, the more real it becomes.

Breaking 80 on a golf course. On the 18th hole on a recent round, I faced a second shot that required hitting the ball low to stay under a tree and rolling it up onto the green. The smart decision was a longer club, a smooth swing, no heroics. The ball stayed under the tree. It rolled up just past the green. A flat chip shot followed. The birdie putt lipped and stopped a foot away. Tap-in par. 80.

The inner critic was not present on that hole. My mind was too occupied with creative problem-solving: how do I get myself into position to make birdie. There was no space left over for doubt. That is the keyword operating in the present tense: self-discipline applied to a single shot, in a moment too full for the critic to find a gap. Eighty is close enough now to feel real instead of theoretical.

Golf has been part of my life for most of it. I played competitively when I was younger and have carried the game through every season that followed. For the past decade, the body made the game harder than it once was. Now the practice is building the body back toward the game. Breaking 80 is not just a score. It is the convergence of everything the hope practice has been building: the physical capability, the mental presence, the discipline applied to a single decision under pressure. The 18th hole that day was not a victory. It was a proof of concept. Pebble Beach is the next one.

## Why the Brain Needs a Specific Picture

The reason specificity matters is not aesthetic. It is neurological.

Dr. Jane McGonigal's research in neuroscience surfaced something counterintuitive: when you imagine your future self, your brain treats that person as a stranger.[¹⁴] MRI studies show neural patterns similar to imagining someone you have never met. This is why it comes so easily to make decisions today that harm the person you will be tomorrow. That person is not real enough yet to protect.

The solution McGonigal identifies is Episodic Future Thinking: mentally simulating specific future experiences in vivid, sensory detail. Not "I want to be healthy someday." A specific room. A specific face. A specific feeling. A specific moment close enough to touch in imagination. The more vivid and concrete the simulation, the more real the future self becomes. As that person becomes real, present-day decisions shift in their direction.

Pictures is a personal future simulation compressed into a single word. When I say it, I am not thinking abstractly about health goals. I am seeing the tux. I am seeing Debby's dress. I am feeling the tap-in par landing in the cup. The future self stops being a stranger and becomes someone worth getting out of bed for at 5 am, before the argument starts, before the inner critic finishes its opening line.

The keyword works because it is never vague. Vague hope stays in the future. Specific hope arrives in the present as fuel.

There is a version of this principle I learned long before I heard McGonigal's name. Jeff Sutherland built Scrum around the idea that when work is visible, it becomes far easier to act on it.[¹²] A team staring at an abstract backlog stalls. A team looking at a specific, visible board, with the actual next task named and in front of them, moves. I have watched that play out in conference rooms for years. It turns out the same rule governs a single person on a hard morning. An abstract goal is a stalled backlog. A specific picture, the tux, the dress, the tap-in par, is the task named and put in front of you. Visibility is not a productivity trick. It is how any mind, in a boardroom or in bed at 5 am, decides to move.

The practical method matters here. Episodic Future Thinking is not a passive exercise. It is specific and sensory. The goal is to simulate a future experience with enough detail that the body responds as if it is partially real. What room holds the moment. What are you wearing. Who is standing next to you. What does the light look like. What does your body feel capable of doing in that moment that it cannot do right now.

The simulation works best when it includes something physical: the handshake at a graduation, the arm around a grandchild, the club at the top of a backswing, the shoulder rolling on a dance floor. A future self doing something, not just standing somewhere, becomes real faster. The more specific the action, the more the present-day body recognizes the investment as worthwhile.

That is what the keyword compresses. Not just a name for the category. A doorway into the simulation, accessible in under a second, on any morning, under any conditions. Saying the word is the first step of the simulation. The body knows the rest.

## The Receipt That Was Already There

Something unexpected happened later in the practice. The keyword, which I had built entirely as a forward-looking tool, started working backward.

At my mother's visitation, a photo loop played near the entrance. Decades of family photographs. At some point I walked past and caught a few photos of myself in the loop: an older, heavier version, standing in gatherings I remember well. I didn't stop. The day had too much else happening.

Something registered quietly as I moved through the room. The distance between the person on that screen and the person walking through that room was real. In that quiet recognition, something that felt like hope arrived, not from imagining the future, but from seeing what the practice had already built.

That was a new layer of the keyword I hadn't found before. I had built Pictures entirely as a forward-looking word: the future photographs, the grandchildren who don't exist yet, the wedding, the harness closing with room to spare on a theme park ride. I hadn't considered that it worked backward too. Old photographs hold the starting point that makes the destination meaningful. The camera doesn't revise the past the way the inner critic does. It just shows what was there.

The deepest version of this arrived later, looking at a photograph of my father in his Air Force dress khaki uniform: lean, sharp, receiving a commendation, my mother beside him healthy and beautiful, a young version of me in the frame too. The feeling wasn't grief. It was pride. He was that person, before the years and the long decline took so much. The keyword traces back to that photograph as much as it points forward to the wedding I haven't attended yet.

John Shedd captured it this way: "Ships are safe in harbor, but that's not what ships are built for."[²³] The hope in that photograph, the version of my father in his uniform, was always there. It reads as inspiration rather than loss because the keyword taught me how to read it. The same evidence looks different depending on which direction you face. Face forward and the harbor feels safe. Face the open water and the harbor looks like the wrong choice.

Hope doesn't only live in the future. It lives in the distance already traveled, in the receipts already gathered. The future is where hope points. The past is where it already proved itself.

The concept worth naming here is borrowed hope. Your past self, the version that existed before the practice had a name, before the word arrived, before the receipts accumulated, was already doing something. Getting up each morning. Carrying the load. Showing up for the people who needed it while running on less than the present self now carries.

Looking back at that person is not pity. It draws hope from someone who had fewer reasons for it. The past self built the foundation the present self stands upon. The present self owes something to that effort, and the way to honor it is to keep building forward. The keyword connects both ends of that thread. It knows what the past self was reaching for, and what the future self still needs. Saying it in the morning is a conversation across time.

## When the Word Stops Being Enough

For several years before February 2025, my North Star was "be a light."

The North Star is the output of the Agile Best Self North Star Workshop, a program Michaele Gardner and I built and have delivered to organizations since 2020. Agile Best Self is a mindset for applying agile principles to personal growth and self-care. The workshop culminates in what we call a cereal box, a short personal declaration that meets Gervais's three criteria: under 25 words, speakable under duress, felt somewhere in the body when you say it. My cereal box said: be a light.

That declaration made sense when I chose it. It described what I wanted to offer others. Lighting the path. Making things clearer for the people around me. It was outward-facing and generous and true.

Over time I recognized it was also too passive. The same relationship exists between optimism and hope: optimism is a disposition toward the future, a stance, something you hold. Hope requires action. "Be a light" described what I provided, not who I was becoming. It pointed outward rather than inward. It described a service, not an identity.

The hope practice demanded more than that.

"Strong and majestic" replaced "be a light." The new declaration describes who I am becoming, not what I offer. Active. Identity-based. "Be a light" described a service. Pictures is how I connect with what that looks like in practice: the specific future moments where being strong and majestic becomes visible in a photograph. The North Star points at the being. The keyword points at the evidence of the being.

This is Agile Best Self Principle 8: being more over doing more. The hope practice does not ask what you will accomplish. It asks who you are becoming. Those are different questions with different answers, and only one of them produces a keyword with enough weight to carry you through the hard morning.

If your keyword fades, you found a goal, not a cause. Goals reach their horizon and go quiet. A cause that is genuinely larger than you keeps you moving, not because discipline holds, but because the people and moments inside it matter enough to sustain the effort. The practice doesn't maintain the keyword. The keyword maintains the practice.

The recognition was gradual rather than sudden. "Be a light" stopped working slowly, the way a keyword erodes when the practice outgrows it rather than fails it. The word still sounded right when I said it. The physical response Gervais describes, the sensation in the spine and across the shoulders, had quieted. The cereal box was producing something closer to a nod than a response.

That quieting deserves attention. The body knows before the mind does. When the keyword stops landing the way it once did, the practice has not failed. It has grown. The cause that was large enough at one stage of the journey needs to deepen to carry the next one.

## Now Find Yours

Your keyword is not Pictures. Your just cause is yours to name.

The work of this chapter is not to adopt this word or these scenes or this particular architecture of future moments. It is to find the one that meets the same criteria for you: specific enough to produce a physical response when you say it, vivid enough to make your future self real enough to protect, large enough to survive contact with a bad morning and still pull you forward.

Start with the same question I would ask anyone who tells me they can't find theirs. Not what do you want to accomplish. Who are you? Describe yourself, not your goals. Then run that answer through the same test Gervais built the cereal box on: get it under 25 words, make it something you can say under duress, and check whether you feel it somewhere in your body when you say it out loud. That last part is not optional. If nothing moves in your chest or your spine when you hear yourself say it, it is not the word yet. Keep cutting.

Most people start with a goal. That is fine. Goals are a starting point. Once the goal is in place, the next question is what lies underneath it. What is the just cause that the goal is protecting? If the goal is to get healthier, what does that health make possible? If the goal is to finish a project, what does finishing it mean for the people who need what it contains? The just cause is usually one layer deeper than the goal. When you find it, you feel it, and that physical response is the signal that you have found the right word.

Do not wait for the perfect word before you start using one. I learned a version of this from Jeff Sutherland's work long before I applied it to my own mornings: the cost of delay is real, and a fast, imperfect decision is usually worth more than a slow, perfect one that arrives too late to matter.[¹²] The same is true here. A rough keyword you actually say at 5 am beats a perfect one you are still refining on paper. Pick one. Say it tomorrow morning. Sharpen it as you go. If it turns out wrong, you will feel that too, and that feeling is data, not failure.

If your keyword fades after a few weeks, that is useful information. It means you found a goal, not a cause. Go one layer deeper. Ask again. The cause is there. It takes the practice time to surface it.

And it is fine to borrow mine for a morning while you look for your own. Say Pictures. Notice where it doesn't quite fit, what scene it fails to produce for you, what physical response it doesn't give you. Sometimes the fastest way to find your own word is standing inside someone else's for a while and feeling exactly where it comes up short.

Expect the keyword to evolve, too. What the word means in the first month of the practice is not what it will mean in the second year. New layers arrive. The word starts working in directions you didn't design. The past shows up in it alongside the future. The people inside it become more specific, more real, more worth the daily sacrifice.

One honest note for the reader whose future self comparison shows something harder. Not growth. Decline. A body that will not recover in the same direction. A season of loss with no obvious forward path. The keyword for that person does not point at a photograph that doesn't exist yet. It points at something still possible: one more day of presence, one more act of meaning, one more choice that says this is not how it ends. The just cause for that person is not a future they will reach. It is the person they are still becoming in the time still available. That cause is just as large. It deserves just as specific a word.

That is the keyword maturing. Let it. A just cause that grows with the practice is proof that the practice is working.
