# Chapter 3: The 90 Percent

*Third Draft — May 27, 2026*

---

My father stopped sleeping in a bed years before he died. At some point during his Alzheimer's decline, his sense of day and night reversed. He would be awake at 3 am, restless and confused, and then asleep in his chair through the afternoon. The circadian rhythm that organizes everything, the hormone cycles, the memory consolidation, the cellular repair, had simply failed. A man who had always been up early, engaged and present, was now untethered from time.

I don't know exactly when I lost the ability to have a real conversation with him. The decline was gradual enough that there was no final exchange I could identify as the last one. It just slowly became impossible. The man who would have gotten the second phone call when something good happened in my life, the promotions, the job changes, the problems that finally broke the right way, needed prompting to remember that I was his son. He was still kind, still social, still himself in certain surface ways. That particular kind of presence he had, the one that understood me, was gone before he was.

In 2006, after his mother died, he sat down with me and asked me to be a better steward of what he and my mother had built together. He was direct and serious, fully himself. He needed me to show up when he couldn't. We went through everything together: where it was, what mattered, what his priorities were. Top of mind: make sure my mother had everything she needed if he went first. He handed me that responsibility while he still had the capacity. I carried it for nearly twenty years.

It felt more like an honor than a burden. I could see from the look on his face how serious he was, and I received it with the same weight he gave it. This was his life's work, his and my mother's, and he needed to know it wouldn't be foolishly spent. I owed him that. I owed my family that. So I said yes, and I meant it, and I carried it until the day the last item was closed and the work was done.

The hope practice that began in February 2025 was always partly an answer to what happened to him. Not the grief, though that was real. The fear. The specific, honest fear that I was watching a trajectory and needed to decide whether to follow it.

---

Dr. Sanjay Gupta, a neurosurgeon and one of the most respected voices in brain health, documents something in *Keep Sharp* that stopped me when I first encountered it: roughly 90 percent of cognitive decline is lifestyle-related, not genetic.[¹⁰] Only about 10 percent is determined by DNA. The rest is shaped by the choices made every day: what you eat, how you move, how you sleep, who you spend time with, and whether you keep challenging your brain with new learning.

That number doesn't promise anything. My father's outcome is not guaranteed to be mine. Nothing in the research says that. What it does say is that the odds are shapeable. The lifestyle choices available to any person willing to make them shift the probabilities in a measurable direction. That's not magical thinking. It's the honest version of what the hope practice actually offers: not control over the result, but influence over the conditions.

I had already heard the principle from Dr. Lee before I ever read Gupta's book. Dementia risk, he told me, is more modifiable than most people assume. What Gupta did was give that idea shape and language. That didn't erase the fear. The risk is not zero, and I know that clearly. What it did was give the fear a direction. Every morning I show up is a morning I am lowering the risk rather than surrendering to it. The confidence that comes from that isn't wishful thinking. It's a receipt the hope practice generates, one session at a time.

Here is the surprising truth this chapter rests on: building a hope practice isn't just good for your soul. It is good for your brain. A better brain builds better hope. The connection runs in both directions, which is why the physical infrastructure of the hope practice matters as much as the mental and emotional work. This chapter is about that infrastructure: exercise, sleep, nutrition, and social connection, the four pillars that support not just a healthy body, but a mind capable of sustaining hope.

---

Exercise was the first pillar because it changed my brain fastest. Dr. John Ratey, a clinical professor of psychiatry at Harvard Medical School, makes a case in *Spark* that changed how I think about every morning workout.[¹¹] The case is this: aerobic exercise physically remodels the brain. Not metaphorically, not in some general mood-improvement sense, but structurally, measurably, at the cellular level. The mechanism is BDNF, brain-derived neurotrophic factor, which Ratey describes as "miracle-gro for the brain." Exercise stimulates BDNF production. BDNF supports the growth and strengthening of neural connections and is associated with better alertness, attention, and motivation. A brain that exercises regularly is biologically different from one that doesn't.

I did not know any of this when I walked into the gym for the first time in February 2025. What I knew was that the commitment was one day, and that getting there felt like the only thing on the agenda. The neuroscience arrived later, as confirmation rather than motivation. The hope practice came first. The understanding of why it worked followed.

What surprised me in those early months was the speed. The weight started coming off faster than I had expected, and that early momentum did something the inner critic hadn't accounted for: it made me want to keep going even more. Not because I needed the motivation, the commitment was already made, but because the receipt arrived early and it was larger than anticipated. The snowball had started rolling. Each session built on the last. Each scan confirmed the direction. The hope practice wasn't just working. It was working faster than the voice in my head had predicted it would, and that gap between what the critic said was possible and what the data showed was actually happening became its own kind of fuel.

What I can tell you from the inside is this: since February 2025, I have always, always, always felt better when I leave the gym. Without exception. What "feel better" means is all of it at once: more energy, better mood, greater clarity, steadier confidence. Some mornings I walk in already carrying the weight of the day ahead, already questioning whether I can rise to a particular challenge. That mindset shifts after the workout, most of the time. The probabilities for everything else that day increase dramatically once the session ends.

For me, the gym doesn't just build the body. It resets the mind before the day has a chance to set the terms. A day that starts there begins with a receipt. A day that doesn't may need to find that receipt somewhere else.

The morning training routine isn't, for me, a vanity project or a fitness goal dressed up as something more important. It's a brain investment. Every session is a deposit into the account that funds clearer thinking, steadier mood, and the cognitive capacity to keep choosing hope when the inner critic is making its case. Ratey's research explains why. The experience confirms it.

---

Sleep was the hidden pillar because it determined whether the rest of the system could do its work. Sleep is the brain's maintenance window, when it does essential overnight repair and reset work, and most people don't treat it that way.

During sleep, the brain does work it cannot do while you're awake. Memory consolidates, meaning the day's experiences are stored more durably. Cellular waste clears. Hormone cycles regulate. The immune system strengthens. Disrupting that window doesn't just make you tired. It compromises the very infrastructure the hope practice requires. A brain running on poor sleep is a brain running on reduced capacity, which makes everything harder: the choices, the focus, the resilience, the ability to regulate the inner critic's volume.

I have paid attention to sleep for most of my adult life, partly because of what I watched happen to my father. His circadian rhythm didn't collapse overnight. It eroded, gradually, as the disease progressed, until the maintenance window closed entirely. He slept in a chair for years because he no longer knew when he was supposed to be awake. I don't know whether poor sleep contributed to his decline or was a symptom of it. What the research suggests is that the relationship runs in both directions. Sleep deprivation increases the risk of cognitive decline, and cognitive decline disrupts sleep.

About fifteen years ago, I was diagnosed with sleep apnea, a condition in which breathing repeatedly stops or becomes shallow during sleep, and started using a CPAP machine to keep the airway open. In the 2025 health journey, once my face shape changed enough that the mask no longer fit correctly, Debby and I decided to see whether I could manage without it. With some anti-snoring strategies in place, I have been able to do that. The CPAP removal is one of the receipts I carry from this journey: a piece of medical equipment made unnecessary by a practice that changed the underlying conditions.

The Whoop sensor came later and opened a new layer. Looking at the actual data, heart rate variability, the degree of variation between heartbeats that often reflects recovery and stress levels, sleep stages, and recovery scores, created opportunities to run experiments that intuition alone couldn't have produced. The most impactful one: stopping caffeine intake around 1 pm. That single change produced a measurable result on roughly 75 percent of nights. Sleep quality improved. The body's maintenance window opened more reliably.

There's also a failure mode worth naming, because the inner critic is at its most creative in the quiet hours. The scenario is specific: waking at 4:30 am, not wanting to disturb Debby by going back to bed, and the couch presenting itself as the considerate choice. The TV is there. The inner critic's argument sounds almost noble. The couch wins when the TV gets turned on, and what follows isn't rest. It's circadian disruption, the whole day set back in energy and focus. The TV isn't laziness. It's the most reasonable-sounding trap in the hope practice.

The solution wasn't willpower. It was engineering. Removing the conditions that made the trap plausible: the caffeine timing, the sleep hygiene habits, the Whoop data creating accountability to something measurable. The couch wins less often now. The last time it happened is hard to remember.

---

Nutrition taught me to notice, not just consume. It changed how I think, not just how I look.

The shift started gradually, as most of the important shifts in this journey did. The more attention I paid to what I was eating, the more I noticed the connection between specific foods and how my mind felt afterward. Not just energy levels. Actual cognitive clarity, or the absence of it. That awareness became the feeling I sought. It made choices easier, especially in restaurants. The question changed: "what sounds good?" became "what makes me feel good?" Those are different questions with different answers, and learning to ask the right one is a cognitive skill in its own right.

The food window, the core structure of intermittent fasting, had already been introduced on that drive home in February 2025: the podcast that made the concept sound less dramatic than the term suggested. What I discovered over the following months was that the timing mattered as much as the content. Eating within a defined window, stopping food after dinner, giving the digestive system a long overnight break, these weren't restrictions. They were conditions that made the brain work better the next morning.

The structure of the day is deliberate. Training happens in a fasted state, timed to maximize fat burning. That was Dr. Lee's recommendation, not my own intuition. The fast runs roughly 17 to 18 hours, broken at lunch. Dinner starts the next fast. Caffeine stops around 1 pm to protect the sleep window. On strength training days, a post-workout protein shake ends the fast early and supports muscle recovery. The whole system is designed to work together, each element reinforcing the next.

What makes it sustainable is the flexibility I built into it. Once a month I have breakfast with a few members of my inner circle, men I trust, who know me well, and who between us manage to solve most of our collective problems over eggs and occasionally create a new one or two. That meal matters more than the fast. Occasional social meals don't shut down progress. The data has confirmed that over and over. The long-range mindset allows for living. That design choice is not a concession. It's the reason the hope practice has lasted. A system that requires perfection doesn't survive contact with real life. A system that builds in room for what matters does.

The learning arrived through contrast. Onion rings make me feel terrible. A side salad in their place makes a noticeable difference. That's the whole nutritional philosophy in one restaurant decision. Proteins in moderation feel good. Green vegetables feel good. Sweet potatoes became a staple. More recently, peanuts came off the list. I had been eating them to hit protein targets, but the sodium and fat load didn't serve the hope practice. The protein goal was right. The vehicle was wrong. Alcohol was the clearest signal of all: even one beer impacts sleep quality in a way the Whoop data makes visible. Zero beers is better. An NA beer covers the social experience on the occasions when that matters.

Of all the cognitive improvements in the hope practice, mindfulness improved the most for me. By that I mean the ability to notice what was happening in my body and mind, rather than operating on autopilot. Not just hunger and fullness, but whether I felt clear or foggy, energized or flat. That noticing is a skill that developed over months of paying close attention. The hope practice built the observer.

---

Connection made the whole hope practice sustainable. Dr. Gupta dedicates a chapter of *Keep Sharp* to a concept he calls "connection for protection," and the evidence he marshals is substantial.[¹⁰] The research is clear: social connection is as essential to brain health as exercise, sleep, and nutrition. Isolation is a cognitive risk factor. Loneliness can accelerate decline. The relationships that hold you, challenge you, and witness your effort are doing biological work, not just emotional work.

The circle, Debby, Kaylee, Dr. Lee, and the others who held the hope practice together, wasn't just emotional support. It was infrastructure. The creatine story is one illustration: Kaylee found the route, Dr. Lee validated it, the hope practice required both of them. Neither of them knew they were contributing to brain health when they played their parts. They were just doing their jobs. That's how We Power works. The community's contribution is often invisible to the contributors.

There is a harder illustration. In October 2025, I sat in a hospital room with my mother and a nurse practitioner as my mother made the decision to transition to hospice care. The nurse practitioner was experienced and skilled. She asked simple, leading questions that let my mother find her own answer rather than being handed one. My mother knew what she wanted. She wanted to go home and knit monkeys for as long as she could, without pain. That was the answer. Clear and honest and entirely her own.

She turned to me for my opinion. My heart was breaking. What I gave her was steadiness. That's what she needed, and steadiness was what the hope practice had built. Not the absence of grief, but the capacity to hold grief and still show up clearly for someone who needed me present. The Hope Cycle was running in that room. Not building toward something better. Recognizing what mattered and releasing what couldn't be changed. The circle held because I had been building the hope practice that made holding possible.

The nameless gym community is a quieter version of the same thing. The same people at the same time, most of them unknown to each other by name. Their presence establishes an unspoken standard. You don't want to be absent when the others arrive. That accountability costs nothing and produces more than its weight in consistency.

Connection is not, in my experience, a luxury add-on to the hope practice. It is foundational.

---

A brief word on supplements, because they are part of the infrastructure and deserve honest treatment.

The goal was specific: maintain and grow lean muscle mass. After 50, that becomes harder to do through diet and training alone. Targeted supplementation helped close the gap. This wasn't optimization for its own sake. It was maintenance of the physical infrastructure the hope practice requires.

Creatine monohydrate was introduced in Chapter 2 as the unexpected pathway that protected muscle mass during fat loss. It also mattered here for a second reason: the same line of research that makes it useful for muscle function also suggests possible cognitive benefits for older adults.[⁹] The point, for me, was not chasing an edge. It was supporting both body and brain with something simple enough to sustain.

The rest of the protocol came together through my own research and a conversation with Dr. Lee in November 2025, when he supported the broader approach. The details mattered less than the function. I wanted support for muscle preservation, recovery, connective tissue, and inflammation control so the training could continue without interruption. The hope practice depends on consistency, and consistency is easier when the body has what it needs to keep showing up.

The timing mattered too: all of it went into a post-workout drink, mixed and consumed while I was still at the gym. Training and recovery happened in the same place, before the day had a chance to scatter my attention. It also tasted good, which turns out to matter. The easier something is to repeat, the more likely it becomes part of the hope practice rather than an occasional burst of effort.

The InBody scan is the receipt. Muscle mass trending upward while body weight came down is the data that confirms the system is working. Not just the creatine. The whole protocol together.

None of these replace the hope practice. They support the conditions the hope practice requires. The system approach matters: supplements are one variable in a larger ecosystem, not a shortcut or a substitute.

---

By the time I walked into Dr. Lee's office in May 2025, the cognitive shift was already underway. The monitoring tools that had been essential in the early months, the app notifications, the food logging, the constant data tracking, had started to feel less necessary. The hope practice was internalizing. The scaffolding was coming down because the internal structure had taken its place.

My head felt lighter. That's the most accurate description I have. The physical lightness of carrying forty fewer pounds was real too. There was also a cognitive lightness. The overhead of managing a new system had decreased because the system had become a way of life. The inner critic still showed up, still made its case, still tried to find the gap between what I intended and what I actually did. It had less room to operate. A brain that sleeps better, moves more, eats with intention, and stays connected to people worth being accountable to is not the same brain that was waiting for hope to arrive on its own.

The behavioral changes were concrete. In January 2025 I would not run on a treadmill. The weight put too much stress on my knees, and the thought of trying felt like an invitation to fail. By May, the combination of fat loss and the strength the training had built around the joint made it possible. That wasn't just a fitness milestone. It was a receipt that the body was changing in ways that opened new doors rather than just closing old ones.

The other shift was harder to measure but easier to feel. Hard things stopped getting pushed. Problems that would have sat on the list for days got handled right away. The procrastination that had been a quiet tax on my energy for years started to lift. The hope practice wasn't just building hope. It was building the version of me who could do something about the things that mattered.

That's the 90 percent: a probability shift made available through daily choices, one morning at a time.

---

That does not mean the path will look the same for everyone, or even that everyone begins from the same position. Some people face chronic conditions, caregiving responsibilities, financial constraints, or histories that make the starting line look very different. I recognize that. The privilege of time, resources, and support that made this hope practice possible for me is not equally distributed. The tools look different for different people. The hope practice doesn't.

The point is not to copy my system. The point is to ask, honestly and without self-contempt, what is possible in your life right now that would move the probabilities in a better direction. Sometimes that's a morning workout. Sometimes it's a single walk. Sometimes it's one conversation with one person who sees you clearly. The 90 percent is not a single path. It's a direction.

If there is one thing I would say to the person who sat with a hard number on a doctor's screen in January 2025, it would be this: just start. You already have everything you need. Not the perfect plan, not the ideal circumstances, not the complete understanding of why it will work. Just enough to take one step. The hope practice builds from there. The receipts arrive. The brain catches up. The inner critic gets quieter, not because you silence it, but because the evidence starts to outweigh its argument.

Be kind to yourself along the way. This work is hard. There will be days that don't go well, mornings the couch wins, meals that weren't the right choice, sessions that felt like they cost more than they gave. That's not failure. That's the hope practice being honest about what it requires. The measure is not perfection. It's direction.

The brain you have in ten years is not entirely predetermined. It is being shaped right now, by what you do tomorrow morning and the morning after that. Nothing is guaranteed. Influenced, though. Meaningfully, measurably influenced.

The hope practice doesn't just build hope. It builds the brain that sustains hope when everything else is trying to drain it. That's not a minor benefit. That's the whole point.

Ask yourself one question before the next morning begins: what does my system need today to give it the best shot? The answer changes. The question stays the same.

---

*End of Chapter 3*
