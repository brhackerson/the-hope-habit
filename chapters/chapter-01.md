# Chapter 1: The Lie We Tell Ourselves About Hope

*Second Draft — May 24, 2026*

---

For most of my life, I thought hope was something that showed up when conditions were right. A feeling. A mood. Something the naturally optimistic carried around in abundance while the rest of us waited for our turn, watching for the right set of circumstances to arrive before we allowed ourselves to believe things might actually get better. I never questioned that assumption. It felt like common sense — like hoping was something you did after the situation warranted it, not before.

That assumption cost me more than I understood at the time.

The pandemic hit in March 2020 and I did what a lot of people did: I watched the news. Hours of it, day after day, waiting for something good to happen. The dread settled in quietly at first, manageable in small doses, something I could set aside when the day demanded it. Then it became constant. I stopped expecting things to improve. That shift wasn't a decision I made consciously — it was a slow drift into a new normal where hope just stopped showing up and I stopped noticing it was gone. That's the insidious thing about losing hope gradually. You don't feel it leave. You just find yourself one day operating without it, and the absence has become so familiar it no longer registers as a loss.

The absurdity of that period still gets me. At one point, someone mentioned a run on toilet paper and suddenly there was low-grade panic in our house. The plan changed immediately — we needed to find whatever supply we could before it was gone. Looking back it almost seems funny, the kind of story you tell at a dinner party and everyone laughs because they lived some version of it too. But in the moment it felt completely reasonable, even urgent. That's what the absence of hope does to a person. It collapses the space between what happens and how you react, eliminating the pause where judgment and perspective normally live. Everything feels urgent. Everything gets a response it doesn't deserve, because you no longer have the reserves to sort the trivial from the serious.

The lie I had believed my whole life was exactly that posture: if you wait long enough and the circumstances improve enough, hope will eventually arrive on its own. It doesn't work that way. Hope doesn't show up because things got easier. You build it, or you don't have it.

---

I want to be clear about something before going further. My life in the years leading up to this journey was genuinely good in most of the ways that matter. My work was going well. My family was healthy and close. I had relationships I valued and a life I was proud of in most of its dimensions. I am not telling a story of someone who had lost everything and had nowhere to go but up.

What I had lost, quietly and without making a conscious decision about it, was my health. Not dramatically — I wasn't in a crisis, I wasn't incapacitated — but consistently, in the way that happens when you care deeply about everything except yourself. Work got a lot of my energy. My wife Debby and the rest of the family got plenty of it too — I was present, I was engaged, I cared deeply about all of it. The thing that kept getting pushed aside was me. My own wellbeing kept getting pushed to the back of the line because everything else felt more urgent, more rewarding, more worthy of attention. I was carrying more weight than was good for me, managing it with medication, and choosing clothes that hid rather than fit. I knew it. I just kept finding reasons to deal with it later.

I had actually started trying to change that in January 2020, three months before the pandemic arrived. I was making real progress. Then March happened, and the focus shifted entirely to helping my family navigate something none of us had a map for. My health went back to the back of the line, where it stayed for another five years.

---

Two voices were running in the background through all of it, and understanding them — really understanding them, not just recognizing that they existed — changed the entire trajectory of what came next.

The first is what I call the inner critic. Everyone has one, though most people move through their lives without ever naming it or examining what it actually does. The inner critic is the voice in your head that runs a constant, low-grade commentary on your limitations, your failures, your odds, and your worthiness. It doesn't need evidence to make its case. It doesn't need logic or consistency or even coherence. It just needs silence and enough unguarded time to fill the space with its preferred version of your story. My inner critic had a simple formula it ran on repeat: "You can't..." followed by whatever blank fit the moment. You can't sustain this. You can't pull this off. You can't be the kind of person who does hard things and sticks with them. The blank changed depending on what I was facing. The opener never did.

What makes the inner critic particularly effective is that it doesn't always announce itself. It rarely arrives as obvious self-doubt or dramatic despair. More often it sounds like caution, like reasonable concern, like the voice of someone who just wants to protect you from embarrassment or failure. Sometimes, if you're not paying careful attention, it sounds indistinguishable from wisdom. That's the tell. When the voice in your head is steering you away from something important without giving you a real reason, when the argument is essentially that you shouldn't try because you might not succeed, you're listening to the inner critic pretend to be something it isn't.

The second voice is a close cousin, and the two often work together without you realizing it: imposter syndrome. Where the inner critic runs as background noise, imposter syndrome activates in specific moments — usually when you step into something new, something larger than what you've done before, something that puts you in front of people who could judge whether you belong. Imposter syndrome whispers that the success you've had wasn't really earned, that the breaks you caught were luck you didn't deserve, that the people around you are more capable and more legitimate and it's only a matter of time before someone figures out the gap between who you appear to be and who you actually are. It is particularly skilled at choosing its moments, showing up precisely when you can least afford the distraction.

I felt both of these voices clearly during this journey. The inner critic filled my quiet early mornings with reasons not to start, cycling through every version of why today wasn't the right day and why I wasn't the right person. Imposter syndrome arrived the moment I considered telling anyone what I was doing, or writing about it publicly, or entertaining the idea that anything I had learned might be worth sharing with someone else. The truth is that I am not a psychologist. I am not a clinical researcher. I don't hold a degree that makes me a credentialed authority on hope or behavior change or the science of human motivation. What I have is a practice that worked for me, and enough time inside that practice to understand why it worked and what it required.

That's what this book is built on. Not a credential or a title or an academic framework, but a practice lived out over months and years, tested against real adversity, and refined through a lot of failure and a lot of getting back up. If you're looking for a textbook, this isn't it. If you're looking for something that might actually help, keep reading.

Here's what both voices have in common, and why naming them matters: they are wrong far more often than they are right. The inner critic mistakes caution for wisdom. Imposter syndrome mistakes unfamiliarity for unworthiness. Learning to recognize them — not to silence them, because that's not realistic, but to stop letting them make your decisions for you — is one of the most important things the hope practice makes possible.

---

C.R. Snyder spent decades studying hope as a psychological construct and arrived at a definition that reframes everything most of us think we know about it. Hope, Snyder argued, is not an emotion — it is cognitive, a motivational state built on two distinct engines. The first is agency: the belief that you have the capacity to move forward, that you are not simply a passenger in your own life. The second is pathways: the ability to find or create a route toward your goal when the obvious one is blocked, the willingness to look for another way when the first way closes. Neither engine is passive. Both require active engagement. Hope is not a feeling you wait for in the hope that it will eventually arrive. It is something you build, deliberately, through the choices you make and the actions you take.[¹]

That distinction matters in ways that are easy to underestimate until you actually sit with it. If hope is a feeling, then your job is to wait for the right conditions. If hope is a practice, then your job is to start building it, right now, with whatever you have.

Most of us have spent our whole lives confusing hope with optimism, treating them as interchangeable when they are actually quite different things. My father called himself an eternal optimist, and he was — genuinely, consistently, and without apology. Through some of the hardest years I watched our family navigate in the 1980s, including two mortgages, a job loss, and a significant career transition, he never lost his fundamental belief that things were going to be okay. That belief was real and it held. What didn't hold was his health. The optimism sustained his outlook through difficult circumstances, but it didn't give him a practice. It didn't show him what to do with his body, his sleep, his daily habits. He paid that price over time, slowly and quietly, the way most people pay the prices they don't realize they're accumulating.

Optimism says things will be okay. Hope shows you what to do next.

Dr. Michael Gervais, one of the world's leading performance psychologists, teaches that optimism can be trained — it is a disposition, useful and genuinely worth cultivating, but ultimately a stance toward the future rather than a plan for navigating it.[⁵] Hope operates differently. Hope is active, directional, and tied to specific actions in specific moments. It showed up differently for me than optimism ever did, and the clearest example of that difference came in one of the last moments I would have expected. Shortly after my mother passed, a single word arrived during an Epiphany Sunday service: hope. What I felt in that moment wasn't simply comfort or the softening of grief. My next steps became clear. I needed to understand what hope actually was, what it required, and how to build it into the fabric of my daily life. A pathway appeared in the middle of loss. That's not optimism. That's agency finding a route forward even when the circumstances are hard.

---

Hope is also not the same as grit, and that distinction is worth making clearly because the two concepts often get treated as synonymous.

Angela Duckworth's research on grit describes the combination of passion and perseverance that allows people to stay the course through difficulty and disappointment over long periods of time. Grit is about endurance — the capacity to keep going when the work is hard and the results are slow. It is a real and genuinely valuable quality, and Duckworth's research demonstrates that it predicts success across a wide range of domains.[²] But grit and hope operate differently. Grit says keep going. Hope says keep looking for the way. A person with grit but without hope can spend years grinding in a direction that stopped making sense long ago, sustained by sheer stubbornness but cut off from the flexibility to find a better route. A person with hope but without grit finds a path and then abandons it at the first serious obstacle, never developing the staying power the journey requires. The practice this book describes needs both, but they are not the same thing and treating them as interchangeable leaves you with an incomplete picture.

Mark Manson, in *Everything Is F\*cked*, argues that the loss of hope in modern life comes primarily from two sources: our collective inability to tolerate discomfort, and our deep confusion about what is actually worth hoping for.[³] Both of those ring true. A genuine hope practice requires a level of honesty about reality that positive thinking can't provide and wishful optimism won't sustain. It requires clear-eyed acknowledgment of what is hard, what is true, and what genuinely matters to you — followed by consistent, deliberate action in that direction, even when the action is uncomfortable, especially when it is.

---

Before going further, I want to address something directly: what does it mean to win?

The word shows up in how I think and talk about this practice, and I want to be clear about what I mean by it, because the wrong definition will undermine everything that follows. Senator Raphael Warnock, preaching on Easter Sunday 2026 from Ebenezer Baptist Church, offered a framing that I haven't been able to improve on.[⁴] He said the Resurrection is the original hope practice. The win is not the absence of the tomb. The win is that the tomb does not get the last word.

That framing is wide enough to hold everyone's story. For someone on a recoverable journey — working toward better health, rebuilding a relationship, recovering from a setback that has a path forward — the win looks like momentum. A better result than last time. A morning you showed up when the easier choice was to stay home. A conversation you had the courage to start. For someone facing something permanent and irreversible — a terminal diagnosis, a profound and irrecoverable loss, a condition that no amount of effort will change — the win looks entirely different. It looks like one more day of presence, one more act of meaning, the story continuing even when continuing is hard. The Resurrection doesn't erase the Crucifixion. It says the Crucifixion was not the end of the story.

That is what hope does. It doesn't promise the tomb won't come. It says the tomb does not get the final word, and it invites you to act accordingly.

---

Here is what I want you to walk away from this book believing, if you believe nothing else: you can do hard things when the why is important enough.

I didn't fully believe that about myself before February 2025. The inner critic had been building its case for years, and I had been half-listening long enough that the argument had started to feel convincing. A doctor's visit that month made some things very clear about where I was and where I was headed if nothing changed. The question that followed wasn't a confident declaration or a sudden burst of motivation. It was small and uncertain and honest: what would it look like if I could actually figure this out?

That question is agency in its earliest and most fragile form. Not certainty, not a plan, not the finished version of yourself arriving ahead of schedule. Just a crack in the wall the inner critic had been building, just enough light coming through to make starting feel possible. I didn't know if I could do it. I just decided to find out.

The inner critic argued against it. It usually does. It was wrong, the way it usually is when it matters most.

You don't need to be a natural optimist to build a hope practice. You don't need a particular faith tradition or a specific set of circumstances or resources you don't currently have. You need to be willing to do the work of building the conditions where hope can arrive, and to keep doing that work even on the days when nothing feels like it's moving. And when hope does arrive, when you feel it and recognize it and know it for what it is, you pass it on to someone who needs it. That's how the practice becomes something larger than yourself.

Hope is available in abundant supply to anyone willing to build toward it. The practice is how you get there.

Your journey looks different from mine. The costs you've been carrying are yours, shaped by your history and your circumstances and your particular version of the inner critic's favorite lines. What transfers across all of it is the core truth: waiting doesn't work. The practice does. And you have more of what you need to start than the voice in your head is ever going to admit.

---

*End of Chapter 1*
