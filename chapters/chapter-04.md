# Chapter 4: The Foundation You're Already Standing On

*Second Draft — June 2026*

---

The hope practice didn't come from nowhere. That is what I want you to understand before we go further. By February 2025, when the doctor's visit changed everything, I had been building something for years. I just didn't know it yet.

The foundation wasn't labeled. It didn't look like preparation. It looked like a career pivot I almost didn't make, a training room I almost didn't walk into, a letter my father wrote when I was sixteen that I filed away and didn't think about again for decades. It looked like a collaboration that started as a conference presentation idea and turned into something neither of us expected. It looked like a word that arrived on an ordinary morning and changed how I started every day that followed.

Looking back, the thread is unmistakable. The choices, the relationships, the principles, the moments of discomfort I pushed through rather than turned away from: all of it was pointing somewhere. When the moment came that I needed something solid to stand on, the ground held. That is not luck. That is what accumulated foundation does. The same is true for you, even if you cannot see it yet. This chapter is about what that foundation looked like, and how it got there, yours as much as mine.

---

In June 2017, I walked into a training room at 3M. The co-creator of Scrum was teaching a Product Owner course, and I had to understand the real thing. I had worked with Scrum teams for years. I had even impersonated a Product Owner at one point. I had never gone to the source. I was curious, and curiosity has always been one of the better decisions I've made.

What I found in that room was not what I expected. Dr. Jeff Sutherland and his colleague stood in front of an audience that included a fair number of skeptics. Corporate training rooms have a way of attracting people who have decided in advance that the thing being taught will not apply to them. These two did not perform enthusiasm. They stood in it. They had done the work, and the work had receipts, and the receipts were sitting right there on the slides and in the results and in the quiet confidence of people who know what they built and why it works. That was not performance. That was what it looks like when you have built something real and spent years proving it.

The next day I made a decision. There was an opportunity in front of me. I could lead the agile transformation of the Corporate Research Systems Lab at 3M, one of the largest and most complex research organizations in the world. It was a role that scared me. I had been in the Scrum world for only a matter of weeks in any serious way. I knew enough to know I didn't know enough. The inner critic made its case immediately.

I decided to say yes.

I knew at that moment I was in it. Outside my comfort zone, which is exactly where learning lives. The inner critic's loudness was, by that point, something I had learned to read as a signal. If it wasn't making noise, I probably wasn't growing. The noise meant I was heading somewhere. I was heading somewhere real.

What followed was harder than I anticipated. Leading a transformation inside an organization I knew well meant navigating the political terrain while trying to stay genuinely curious about the work. It meant going back to the source repeatedly, becoming a credentialed trainer in Jeff's Scrum Inc. program, eventually joining that organization as a senior leader. The methodology changed how I saw work, change, and what people can actually sustain.

And through all of it, five values kept showing up: commitment, focus, openness, respect, courage.

These were not team values to hang on a wall and revisit at quarterly retrospectives. They were personal values. They mapped directly onto how I was already trying to live. They gave language to something I had been reaching for without knowing what to call it.

Commitment to showing up, especially on the days when showing up is hard. Focus on what actually matters, not on the noise the inner critic manufactures to keep you safe and still. Openness to what the evidence is telling you, even when it disagrees with your plan. Respect for yourself and the people around you, enough to tell the truth. Courage to act before you feel ready, because feeling ready is often the inner critic's final offer.

I carried those values into the lab transformation. I carry them into the weight room at 5 am. I carry them into this book.

That was June 2017. The hope practice hadn't been named yet. The foundation was already taking shape.

---

I want to take you back further. To July 3, 1985.

My father wrote a letter that summer. He addressed it to his children. He titled it "Things I Have Learned (Sometimes at Great Expense) Over the Past 22 Plus Years of Married Life." It had eleven items. I was sixteen years old when he wrote it.

I remember reading it the first time and filing it away. It felt like the kind of thing a father was supposed to write. I was sixteen. I wasn't listening the way I would later wish I had been.

Most of the items are what you'd expect from a father trying to pass something along before the words get harder to say. Respect your elders. Listen to your spouse. Laugh at yourself. Hang onto the simple pleasures. The last two items were a joke, the kind only he could fully explain, and they told you everything about who he was. He had a way of ending serious things with something that made you smile and shake your head at the same time.

Two items in the middle stopped me when I read them again decades later.

Item 7: "Prepare yourself for new challenges. I can't remember how many times I've agonized the night before an important exam, meeting or decision. As long as I was prepared, the next day usually went like a breeze. Prepare yourself for the challenge and it will go much easier."

Item 8: "Set aside some time for yourself each day. It will help settle the dust and keep the inner peace."

He wrote those words in 1985.

In 2018, Michaele Gardner and I wrote the twelve principles of Agile Best Self. Principle 7: prepare your environment in support of the goals you've committed to pursuing. Principle 8: prioritize being more over doing more.

My father wrote Principles 7 and 8 forty years before I did.

I sat with that for a long time when I first made the connection. The letter had been in a file. The principles were on a website. They were the same thing. He had arrived at those ideas through a different path, through a marriage and a career and a life and a set of mistakes he had absorbed honestly enough to write about. I arrived at them through agile teams and retrospectives and a collaboration with someone who saw patterns I missed. We were talking about the same truth from different directions.

The foundation runs deeper than we usually recognize. Sometimes it was placed there by the people who came before us, quietly, in the middle of summer, in a letter kept in a file and not read again for thirty years.

I think about my father often. He spent his final years losing himself to Alzheimer's. I watched the man who wrote that letter become someone who needed prompting to remember my name. The conversations that had mattered most became impossible, and then he was gone. He passed in 2022. I visit the cemetery occasionally and talk to him, in a manner of speaking, which is as close as I can get to the second phone call now.

That is the shadow that follows this journey. Part of what drives it. The 90 percent of dementia risk that research tells us is modifiable sits in the same conversation as the five Scrum values and the ABS principles and the keyword. All of it points at the same thing. The hope practice is not just about feeling better. It is about staying present for the life still ahead.

He couldn't build the hope practice he deserved. I can build mine. I owe it to him to do exactly that.

---

In 2018, I attended the Global Scrum Gathering in Minneapolis. I had been living inside the Scrum world for only a couple of months at that point. I had taken on the role that scared me. The credentials were starting to accumulate.

Something was still missing. The agile community was full of people who transformed teams and organizations. The retrospectives, the sprint reviews, the daily standups were all designed to make groups of people more effective together. Very few people were asking the harder question. What about the individual? What about the person showing up to those retrospectives, carrying their own backlog of unexamined habits, unaddressed fears, and unasked questions?

That gathering is where I met Michaele Gardner. She describes herself as a connector and catalyst, and she is exactly that. Michaele brings a gift for seeing patterns across people and ideas that most of us miss while standing in the middle of them. We were both in the middle of serious change initiatives at our respective companies, and after talking for a while, we landed on an idea. A collaboration on a conference presentation would give us both room to grow outside our day jobs, a way to grow outside our day jobs. The conversation didn't stay on work for long. We started talking about a shared idea. What if you applied agile principles not to your work, but to your life? What if the same values that made teams inspect and adapt could help individuals do the same?

We called it Agile Self-Care at first. Michaele had an analogy she liked to use to explain it. Think of the oxygen mask on an airplane, the one they tell you to put on yourself before helping others. That is self-care. She didn't want to stop at the mask. She wanted to fly the plane. That was the shift. You move from tending to yourself so you can keep going, to becoming the person capable of taking the controls. So Agile Self-Care became Agile Best Self.

It is a mindset for continuous personal growth, grounded in the values that make agile teams work. You stay transparent about where you actually are, you inspect what is and isn't serving you, and you adapt when the evidence calls for it. You keep improving even when progress is slow and invisible. Agile Best Self is not a framework you follow. It is a way of seeing yourself, with the same honest curiosity you would bring to a team retrospective.

Michaele and I wrote the twelve principles together in the fall of 2018. We submitted a session proposal to the Agile 2019 conference and were not selected.

The inner critic had notes about that.

Then April 2019 changed what we were building.

A local meetup invited us to lead a workshop. It was the first time we delivered the work to a room, still under its original name, Agile Self-Care, at that point. The room was well attended by agile practitioners from across the Twin Cities, many of them consultants whose work was helping organizations find better ways of working and drive change. These were people who had seen frameworks come and go. Michaele was an established name in the local Agile community by then, and most of the room knew exactly who she was. I was the newcomer, largely unknown to the people in those seats. A friendly room, but not an easy one.

Michaele and I led the workshop together. Something happened in that room that neither of us had anticipated. The conversation went past the exercise. People stayed afterward. They asked how they could bring this to their own teams, their own lives, their own people.

Walking out of the venue that night, we both knew. Over seventy people had filled that room. This was no longer a fun collaboration aimed at a conference talk. It was a community asking to be built. We launched the website the very next day and started building the community in earnest. What started in that meetup room has since grown global in reach. Not long after, I was featured in a chapter of a book about organizational agility, a marker of how far the work had come from a conversation at a conference the year before.

We kept going anyway. That decision, made when the external validation didn't arrive, is one of the more important decisions I've made. It would have been easy to conclude that the idea wasn't ready, or that we weren't the right people to carry it, or that the community wasn't interested. All of those thoughts visited. None of them were true. We had built something real, and real things don't require a conference slot to exist.

The validation did come, just not on the timeline we expected. The North Star Workshop was accepted for Agile 2020. Then the pandemic arrived and the conference was cancelled. The session we had built and rehearsed and waited two years to deliver simply did not happen.

The inner critic had notes about that too.

We kept going. Michaele and I developed a second workshop, the Intention Canvas, designed to help individuals translate the values surfaced in the North Star into concrete daily practice. The Intention Canvas was accepted for Agile 2021. The conference was still remote that year, so we delivered it over video to participants scattered across time zones and home offices. It was not the setting we had imagined when we first submitted to Agile 2019. It was the one we got, and the work landed.

The North Star Workshop became our flagship exercise. Sessions followed. Conversations multiplied. The community grew slowly, the way anything real grows, through individual people finding something in it that resonated and bringing it to the next person. By February 2025, when my health journey began, the mindset I had been developing with Michaele for six years was already running in the background. I didn't go looking for a framework to guide my health transformation. I had already been building one without knowing that was what I was doing.

Principle 8 is the one that lives in my daily practice most visibly. It is being more over doing more. I heard a version of this idea from Dr. Michael Gervais years earlier, in a different context. What it does is shift the fundamental question. The question stops being what I will accomplish today and becomes who I am becoming. Not how many reps I completed, but whether I am becoming the person I said I wanted to be.

The inner critic is very happy to offer things to do that prioritize comfort over progress. That is a feature, not a bug. It is doing exactly what it was designed to do. It keeps you safe and familiar and unchanged. Principle 8 breaks that pattern by changing the question entirely. You are not asking whether you should go to the gym. You are asking whether you are becoming the person in those pictures. That second question is much harder to refuse.

---

Which brings me to the word I have not mentioned yet in this book.

Pictures.

Early in 2025, a few weeks into the health journey, I was sitting in my car before work. I needed something that didn't yet have a name. The inner critic was active every morning. Not dramatically loud. Persistently, quietly loud. Stay in bed. You're tired. One day won't matter. What I needed was a response that worked in under a second. Not a motivational system. Not a playlist. One word, accessible the moment I needed it, that reconnected me to why I was doing any of this.

The North Star Workshop is an Agile Best Self exercise built around a method Dr. Michael Gervais developed for high-performance athletes and elite professionals. Gervais teaches that an effective personal philosophy must meet three criteria. It must be twenty-five words or fewer, shorter is better. It must be speakable under duress, at 5 am, when you're half awake and the inner critic is already ahead of you. When you speak it aloud to someone you trust, you must feel it somewhere in your body. Not as a metaphor. As a genuine physical response, the kind that doesn't lie.

I ran through the exercise. I sat with what mattered most. I let myself think past the day and the week and the month, all the way out to the years still ahead. Emily's wedding someday. The grandchildren who don't exist yet but will. Standing at a tee box with enough energy to play the back nine as I once did. Winters somewhere warm with Debby, which we are starting to plan for right now. All of the photographs. The ones that will be taken at all of those moments, documenting who was there, what he looked like, and whether he showed up healthy and present.

What will my kids tell their grandchildren when they look at those pictures? What will my face in those photographs say about the person I chose to become?

The word arrived: Pictures.

I felt it when I found it. In my spine. Across my shoulders. That physical response Gervais described as the test of a real keyword was real and immediate. This was the word. Not a goal. Not a metric. An identity compressed into a single syllable.

A few weeks later, I got a text from Jim. He is a good friend and a trusted member of my circle, a recently retired corporate fitness executive who has been one of the most consistent supporters of this journey. He understands both the physical side and the mindset side of what I have been building, because he has lived both. He didn't know what I had been working on that morning. He was just sharing a memory. The text contained a photograph from our guys fishing trip to Lac Seul in 2023. Me, in my former body.

I sat with that photograph for a long moment.

The timing wasn't planned. Jim didn't know what the word Pictures had come to mean. He was just a friend sharing a fishing trip photo. Pictures was the keyword I had chosen to anchor the hope practice, and it had just arrived literal and real, at the exact moment I needed it most. The keyword found me back. That is what happens when you build a hope practice around something real enough to feel.

The research, it turns out, had already explained why this works. I found the keyword intuitively. The science explains why it was exactly right.

C.R. Snyder spent decades studying hope and identified two engines. There is agency, the belief that you can move forward, and pathways, the ability to find a route when the obvious one is blocked. Neither is passive. Both require action. Pictures serves both simultaneously. It activates agency, giving me a reason to move, and it opens a pathway in the direction of something worth pursuing. When the inner critic chooses comfort, the word reconnects me to my will and my direction in under a second.[¹³]

Jane McGonigal's research in neuroscience surfaced something counterintuitive. When you imagine your future self, your brain treats that person as a stranger. Literally. MRI studies show neural patterns similar to imagining someone you've never met. This is why decisions that harm the person you will be tomorrow feel so easy to make today. Your future self is not yet real enough to protect.[¹⁴]

McGonigal calls the solution Episodic Future Thinking. The method is to simulate specific future experiences in vivid, sensory detail. Not "I want to be healthy someday." A specific room. A specific face. A specific moment, close enough to touch in your imagination. The more vivid the simulation, the more real your future self becomes. Your present-day decisions shift toward that person.

Pictures is a personal future simulation compressed into a single word. When I say it, I am not thinking abstractly about health goals. I am seeing specific photographs. Specific faces. Specific moments still ahead. My future self stops being a stranger and becomes someone worth getting out of bed for at 5 am.

Dr. Sanjay Gupta, in his research on brain health, documents how meaningful purpose activates the brain differently than obligation does. You can get yourself to the gym out of guilt. The neurochemistry of guilt is not the same as the neurochemistry of meaning. When you speak a keyword with genuine conviction, when you feel that physical response Gervais describes, your brain releases something different. The hope practice is not just psychological. Every morning the keyword works, it is also a small act of brain training.[¹⁸]

Not every morning lands the same way. I should tell you that, because you are going to have the morning I am about to describe. There are days I say the word and the response is slow to come. No flood in the spine, no pull across the shoulders, not yet. Just me in the kitchen, half dressed, saying a word to myself while the inner critic gets louder. It does not argue with the word on those mornings. It just turns up the volume on the thing it always wants, which is comfort over growth, the bed over the gym, today over the years still ahead. What I learned is that the feeling is not the practice. The feeling is a gift when it comes on time. When it is slow, the word is still a decision, and I can make a decision before I feel like it. So I say Pictures and I start moving anyway, half dressed, unconvinced. The going is the practice. The feeling almost always catches up, usually somewhere on the drive, and by the time it does I am already on my way.

That is Principle 8 made operational, Gervais's criteria met, Snyder's agency and pathways activated. It is McGonigal's future simulation running in one word, at the moment the inner critic thinks it has won.

The keyword doesn't replace the work. It starts it. Go be the healthy person in those pictures. Everything else follows from that.

---

Chip Conley didn't use the word foundation. The idea lives in everything he teaches.

Conley is an entrepreneur and author who founded the Modern Elder Academy after joining Airbnb at 52 as the oldest person on the leadership team by a significant margin. He came in as the so-called adult in the room, surrounded by people half his age who knew things he didn't about technology and culture and the assumptions of a new generation. What he brought was different. Pattern recognition, emotional intelligence, a long memory for what had worked and what hadn't in organizations he had built over three decades.[¹⁷]

What he discovered in that experience became his framework for what he calls the Modern Elder, someone who arrives at midlife not with obsolescence, but with accumulated wisdom. Someone who has built enough, failed enough, and learned enough to be genuinely useful in a world that often assumes experience is a liability. His core argument is simple. What you have built over decades is not a weight to carry. It is an asset to spend.

I turned 60 in 2025, the same year the hope practice began in earnest. I arrived at that birthday aware of what I had accumulated. The Scrum values had become a personal operating system, and behind them sat six years of an agile mindset built specifically for individual growth and twelve principles field-tested in workshops across multiple industries. There was a father's letter that mapped directly onto the principles I arrived at independently. Now there was a keyword that connected all of it to a visceral reason to show up every morning.

I was not starting from zero. None of us are.

Conley's work names something that most of us feel but rarely say. At 60, the goal is not to pretend the years didn't happen. The goal is to use them. Every hard conversation you navigated. Every role that scared you before you took it. Every relationship that taught you something you didn't know you needed. Every principle you lived before you named it. All of it counts. All of it is available.

The hope practice didn't replace what I had built. It organized it. It gave it direction and urgency. It asked a better question. The measure was no longer what I had accomplished. It was who I am still becoming, and what that person needs from me today.

---

Here is what I want you to take from this chapter.

You probably have more foundation than you think.

Most of us do not inventory our past experience looking for hidden assets. We look at the distance between where we are and where we want to go, and we see a gap. The inner critic is happy to measure that gap for us, loudly and in exhausting detail. It is very good at making the gap feel like the whole story.

The gap is rarely as empty as it looks.

You have values you developed without ever naming them. Experiences that taught you something even when they failed, especially when they failed. People who said things that stayed with you long after they forgot they had said them. Principles you have been living before you ever heard them described as principles. A letter kept in a file somewhere, from someone who loved you, that was already pointing at what you needed long before you knew you needed it.

The work of excavating your foundation is not complicated. It is honest. Sit with the question. What have I been building, without knowing I was building it? Follow the thread back. Find the values that kept showing up even when you couldn't name them. The decisions that felt like instinct but were actually the accumulation of everything that came before. The people who placed something in you and moved on, not knowing it would matter.

The hope practice builds on what is already there. That is not a small thing. That is the entire difference between building something and excavating something. You are not starting over. You are finding what was already there and giving it a direction.

I am aware that not everyone has a letter from a father who knew how to write one. Not everyone has had access to professional development, or a co-creator who challenged them, or a doctor who told them the hard truth at exactly the right moment. The foundation looks different for every person who has one. The work here is not to replicate mine. It is to find yours.

And yours may be smaller than mine, and it will still hold. I have a rich pile to stand on, and I know it, and I know how that can read from where you are sitting. So let me say the quiet part plainly. The method does not require a letter or a career or a co-creator. It works on scraps. One promise you kept when it would have been easier to break it. One teacher who saw something in you for a single afternoon and said so. One time you were certain you would quit and did not. That is foundation. It does not look like much because foundation never does from the inside. The man who poured mine thought he was just writing his kids a note. The smallest true thing you have done is load-bearing. You do not need a deep foundation to stand. You need a real one, and you already have more real than you think.

Look back before you look forward. The foundation is already under you. The work is learning to stand on it.
