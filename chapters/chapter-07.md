# Chapter 7: Autonomy — The System Nobody Else Can Build For You

*Draft: July 2026*

---

Chapter 6 named the reason to keep going. This chapter is about what you actually build once you have it.

A why is not a system. Pictures gets me out of bed. It doesn't decide what I do once I'm up. That decision belongs to something else: the actual plan, built for one specific body, in one specific life, during one specific season. Nobody else can build it, because nobody else lives this exact version of it.

This chapter is about that system: where it came from, who helped shape it without ever owning it, and the moment the data forced a change nobody had seen coming.

---

## Borrowed Systems Don't Hold

Two other times before February 2025, I tried to make a real change.

2016 first. A different doctor gave me a small nudge, nothing urgent, just a suggestion. The approach was simple: work out and eat less. It worked, for a while. I don't remember exactly why it stopped. There was no crisis on either end. It just faded, the way a plan fades when nothing underneath it keeps it standing.

Then January 2020. I started using a popular weight-loss app and lost thirty-five to forty pounds. It worked, genuinely worked, for a while, and it had its own problems well before the world gave it an excuse to fail. Its feedback loops came off as highly judgmental about food choices, and it pushed me to drop my calories harder than felt sustainable. It was easy to obsess over the numbers it put in front of me every day. The results came fast. They didn't feel balanced. Then March arrived, and the pandemic gave the whole thing a much easier way to fall apart. The derailment wasn't sudden. It was gradual. My focus shifted from myself to helping my family survive a genuinely hard season. Real adversity. Hope wasn't very present in those first six months. That's not a failure of willpower. That's what a crisis looks like from the inside. Continuing didn't seem prudent, or at least that was the story I told myself, once the basic plan in life became just to survive.

Neither attempt failed because the program itself was the whole problem. The 2016 plan worked until it didn't need to keep working. The app worked, in its narrow way, until narrow stopped being enough for what the moment actually required. What both attempts had in common wasn't only a flawed system. It was a system that lived outside of me, something I followed rather than something I owned. When life stopped cooperating, nothing internal remained to catch me.

February 2025 was different from the very first day, and the difference wasn't a better program on paper. It was a decision about how to build one. I already knew a lot of ways this wouldn't work for me, and I had no idea yet what would actually stick. So the plan was to commit to just one day, a single sprint, and pay attention to how I felt and what the outcomes were, then tune and adjust quickly. It was going to be custom-made from the beginning, not a formula adopted whole. I knew I'd need a set of real principles eventually, and I knew building those would take a little time. Day one didn't need any of that yet. Day one was simpler: show up, do the work, evaluate, repeat.

A system someone else designs can carry you for a while. It cannot carry you through everything, because nobody built it with your specific body, your specific life, or your specific season in mind. Only you can build that. Nobody else can build it for you.

## The System Built for This Body, This Life, This Season

The actual system starts with environment. Life Time became the anchor, not because of anything symbolic in the name, but because it's a genuinely comfortable place to show up: clean, well-equipped, low-stress, the kind of environment that removes friction instead of adding it.

That wasn't a blind start. I'd worked with personal trainers at 3M's corporate gym years before any of this began, and I arrived at Life Time already knowing how to build a full-body strength plan. That foundation didn't need to come from nothing. I needed to adapt it, refine it against a body that had changed and a season of life that had changed with it.

I rebuilt cardio around one piece of specific advice from Dr. Lee: interval training over straight steady-state work, because the heart rate going up and coming back down does something steady cardio doesn't. That one adjustment reshaped how I trained, not because I read it somewhere, but because it came from someone who knew my chart and gave me something precise enough to actually use. Supplements followed the same pattern: practical guidance, taken from people who knew what they were talking about, applied to a plan that stayed mine to run.

Nutrition took longer to land, and it's the clearest example of building instead of borrowing. I'd been familiar with intermittent fasting before February 2025, and it carried nothing but negative connotations for me. It sounded like pure suffering. I like to eat. Not eating for long stretches never struck me as sustainable.

It turned out I didn't understand what it meant. It was never really about not eating. It was about being disciplined regarding when to eat, timing that discipline against how the body handles food. Time that against a workout, usually in the morning, and fat burning goes up even more. It also solved a problem I hadn't set out to fix: a habit of snacking late at night that wasn't helping anything. Cutting that off did more than the fasting window itself.

I started with twelve hours as a commitment, nothing dramatic. It didn't take long, and it didn't take much effort, to stretch that to sixteen or eighteen. Stack a workout onto the last part of that window and the fat-burning effect compounds on top of it. Somewhere in there I renamed the whole thing, because I never liked the clinical sound of "intermittent fasting." I call it my eating window now. Delay breakfast as long as the day allows. Add protein to that first meal, every time. Stop eating after dinner, as early in the evening as the day allows, though other people's schedules mean I can't always control exactly when that happens. That's held since, with one adjustment added in the spring of 2026, once most of the weight I wanted gone was gone: on strength training days, protein shortly after the workout, to help the muscle actually keep and grow what the training built.

None of it came from a book followed page by page or an app followed screen by screen. I built all of it, piece by piece, around what this particular body needed in this particular season, using someone else's concept as raw material rather than someone else's plan as a script.

## Input Isn't the Same as Ownership

None of this happened in isolation, and that's worth being honest about. Autonomy doesn't mean going it alone. It means staying the one who decides.

Kaylee, a trainer at Life Time, is the clearest example. She's the one who first flagged something in the data that I hadn't noticed myself, a detail the next section covers in full. Dr. Lee is another: not just the doctor who confirmed my numbers were a problem in the first place, but an ongoing voice through training and supplement decisions, someone whose specific advice shaped the plan directly.

Going back further, the trainers I worked with at 3M, Chris, Anna, Sid, and Darcy, built the foundation years before any of this was urgent. I didn't know in 2015 or 2016 that the strength training habits they taught me would matter this much a decade later. They weren't building a hope practice. They were doing their jobs well, and what they taught stuck.

The newest input in that same lineage doesn't have a name I learned in a gym at all. Today, my post-workout review is mostly a screenshot I take from Apple Fitness and upload to a Claude chat on my phone, which functions as my trainer now in a very specific sense. I like to process things out loud, and this lets me do that without wearing out an actual human's patience. What it's good at is exactly what a good trainer is good at: keeping track of data points and noticing trends I wouldn't catch on my own without a lot of manual work, flagging new personal bests, and holding my focus on the long game instead of whatever single number is loudest that day. That last part isn't automatic. I trained it to do that in the background, the same way I once absorbed what Chris and Anna and Sid and Darcy taught me and kept using it long after they'd moved on to other clients.

None of these inputs ran my system. They fed it. Kaylee's flag became a question I took to Dr. Lee, not a decision Kaylee made for me. Dr. Lee's advice became a training change I implemented, not a program he handed me and I followed blindly. The 3M trainers gave me raw material years before I had any idea what I'd eventually build with it. Even the app on my phone only surfaces what's already there in the data. It doesn't decide what happens next. I do.

That's the actual shape of autonomy. It isn't the absence of other people's expertise, human or otherwise. It's the presence of your own final decision. Take the input. Keep the ownership.

## Make It Easier Than Not Doing It

James Clear's core idea is simple enough to miss: behavior follows environment far more reliably than it follows willpower.[¹⁶] Put a person in an environment where the right behavior is the easy behavior, and discipline stops being the main event. It becomes the smaller job of showing up to a place that's already doing half the work.

Life Time isn't a metaphor, and it isn't the hero of this story either. It's a genuinely comfortable, well-equipped, low-stress place, and that ordinariness is the entire point. I don't need extra motivation to go somewhere that already feels good to walk into. The environment removes the friction discipline would otherwise have to fight through every single morning.

That's a rule I try to remember when I judge myself for how hard something feels: if a behavior consistently feels harder than it should, the honest first question isn't what's wrong with me. It's what's wrong with the environment I built around myself. Fix the environment first. Ask about willpower second.

The gym isn't the hero. The practice is. Life Time just makes the practice easier to sustain than not sustaining it, and that difference, multiplied across a thousand mornings, is most of what autonomy actually looks like from the outside.

## The Pivot

There aren't really any failures in this practice. Only more data that tells me what to do next. That reframe took a while to earn, and two moments taught it to me directly.

The first arrived early, when an InBody scan showed something that didn't make sense. Weight was dropping. Muscle mass was dropping too, at the same time. That wasn't the goal. Losing weight while also losing muscle isn't progress. It's a smaller version of the same problem.

I asked Kaylee about it directly. At over fifty, she told me, the body loses lean muscle mass quickly, and creatine can help reverse that. I was skeptical. It sounded like exactly the kind of gym-floor advice you run past an actual doctor before you act on it. So I took it to Dr. Lee and asked him directly: is this safe for me?

The answer came back fast. A resounding yes.

We added creatine. About two weeks later, the next scan told a different story. Weight continued down. Muscle mass, which had been trending the wrong direction, reversed and started climbing. I had trusted what Kaylee told me. I believed Dr. Lee's confirmation. Some part of me still needed to see the receipt before it fully landed. When the receipt arrived, it counted as more than a data point. It was evidence that the route I hadn't planned for, hadn't researched, hadn't even known to look for, was the right one. That was the moment the inner advocate started showing up regularly, not as an occasional visitor but as something closer to a resident. The inner critic didn't disappear. It moved to the back seat, quieter, less in charge.

The second moment arrived over a year later, in the spring of 2026, and it corrected in the opposite direction. I'd shifted focus toward adding lean muscle mass through heavier protein and consistent lifting, aiming for five or six pounds of it over a few months. It was an arbitrary number, chosen more than earned. A month in, I'd dialed the protein up hard and kept the workouts consistent, and the weight I lifted kept gently increasing. Then an InBody scan in May showed the lean muscle progress was real, but my body weight had climbed further than expected, and honestly, I didn't feel nearly as good as the numbers suggested I should. I looked at the data and at how I actually felt, and realized the goal itself was wrong, not the effort behind it. I pulled the protein back a little. The weight settled into a range that felt right, the lean muscle mass held, and my body fat went down as well.

Two things came out of that second correction. First, the sensor inside, how I actually feel, has to trump everything else on the page. Second, real data makes the tweaks simple, because I'm never just trying random things and hoping one of them works.

Neither pivot was dramatic. Both were quiet and specific, and both came from the same habit: taking the input in, testing it against evidence and against how the body actually feels, and adjusting the system when either one says to. That's autonomy in its most concrete form. Not stubbornly refusing outside input, and not treating a number as gospel either. Reading the whole picture, then deciding.

---

## Build Yours

My system is not your system, and it was never supposed to be. The workouts, the interval structure Dr. Lee suggested, the eating window, the creatine, the specific gym with the specific trainers who happened to be there when I needed them: none of it transfers directly to your body, your life, or your season.

What transfers is the shape underneath it. Build an environment that makes the right behavior the easy behavior. Take input from people, and tools, that know things you don't, and keep the final decision for yourself. Watch what the data actually says, and watch how you actually feel, and change course when either one tells you to, even if the change wasn't part of the original plan. If a system starts to feel like judgment instead of information, that's worth noticing. It's usually a sign you borrowed the system a little too completely. Rename what doesn't fit the way you think or talk. You'll eventually abandon a word that sounds like suffering, no matter how sound the idea underneath it is.

If you're borrowing someone else's program right now, a book, an app, a friend's routine that worked for them, that's a fine place to start. It is not a fine place to stay. At some point the plan has to become yours: you adjust it for your body, test it against your own results and your own felt experience, own it instead of just following it.

There are no failures here. Only more data, telling you what to build next.

Nobody else can build this for you. That was true for me in 2016, in 2020, and it's still true now. It's true for you too. The system that will actually hold you through a hard season is the one you build, piece by piece, out of what you learn about your own body and what it actually needs.
