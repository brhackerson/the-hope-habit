# For Anyone Looking for Something to Grab Onto

### This is not a prescription. It's an invitation to find your own version.

---

As of right now, twenty posts into this Substack, a corresponding book project is taking shape, showing a hope practice described in more detail than I ever expected to share publicly — and the most honest thing I can say is this:

## Who cares?

This question popped into my head, served up by, you guessed it, my inner critic.

It is a fair question that deserves a real answer, not a marketing pitch to gain followers or subscribers. This journey to find and share hope and improve my health started as a gift to myself and my family, and it became something outward because the practice demanded it. More people bringing hope into the world during difficult times is good for all of us. The writing exists to share what the hope practice taught, not to build a brand. This post isn't about me. It's about you finding your own version.

## You Don't Have to Have It Figured Out

This post is not for people who have it mostly figured out. It's for the person feeling little or no hope, looking for something to grab onto — the caregiver who has nothing left after months of giving everything to someone else, the person watching the news every morning and feeling like hope is a luxury they can't afford, the person who has tried various self-help practices before, felt good for a few weeks, and watched it fade, and the skeptic who thinks anyone talking about hope either hasn't been paying attention or has something to sell.

This post is for the person in any low: health, financial, relational, the world-is-chaos low that doesn't have a clean name — all of them. If any of that sounds familiar, keep reading.

## Hope Is Not Optimism

For the skeptic, the distinction matters more than almost anything else in this post.

Optimism is a real and valuable skill — Dr. Michael Gervais, one of the world's leading performance psychologists, defines it as the belief that something good is about to happen, and argues optimism is trainable like any other mental capability. More optimism is a good thing. But optimism and hope are not the same thing, and confusing them is where the skeptic's distrust is most justified.

The skeptic watching the news every morning finds it hard to believe something good is about to happen — and right now, that's not an unreasonable perspective. Hope doesn't ask you to believe that. Hope requires the discipline to show up anyway, regardless of what you believe is coming. Psychologist C.R. Snyder's foundational research describes hope as operating through two engines: agency, the belief that you can move forward, and pathways, the ability to find a route when the obvious one closes. Neither of those engines requires believing something good is about to happen, and both are available to someone in the hardest possible season, carrying the heaviest possible load.

Optimism is a good companion to the hope practice. Hope is available even when optimism has run dry. That's what makes it a more sustainable energy source in the long game.

The news is not wrong, and the world is genuinely hard right now. Real things are happening to real people, and hope doesn't argue with that. It asks: given that this is true, what comes next? That's not naive. That's the most honest response available.

The problem isn't only that the news is bad. It's increasingly hard to know what's real. Misinformation travels faster than correction, and the uncertainty it creates is its own kind of hope drain. When you can't trust the information environment, the inner critic fills the space with its own version of events — which is always the worst possible one. Why? To protect you from harm, as designed. The counter-move is not knowing everything. It's developing the discipline to find what can be trusted, act on what can be known, and not let what can't be verified write the verdict on hope.

## The Only Promise This Practice Makes

Get up and try again tomorrow. Never give up. It's not easy, but it's worth the struggle.

That's the whole answer — no program, no steps, no guarantee that this time will be different because of some technique you haven't tried yet. Most self-help asks you to change everything at once and maintain it perfectly. The hope practice is about just showing up tomorrow. A one-day sprint, that's all. The practice builds in permission to fail from the beginning. The receipts accumulate from there, one boring morning at a time, and the inner critic slowly loses the argument it has been winning for years.

The practice doesn't have to look like a gym. It might be five minutes of writing before the house wakes up, a walk around the block, a prayer, a single good meal, a moment of stillness before the phone comes on. Whatever form fits your life and your season counts. The practice doesn't promise the struggle goes away. It promises the struggle is worth it, and that's a promise it can actually keep.

## The Most Important Question in This Post

One question: what's one thing you can do today for yourself before caring for someone else? Not a program, not a gym membership, not a restructured morning routine — one thing, whatever fits your circumstances, your season, your five available minutes.

From July through December 2025, I showed up for my hope practice while also showing up for my mother in the final months of her life. Many days started already exhausted, and it would have been easy and completely justifiable to stop. Choosing to keep going meant one thing: get there. The discovery surprised me — the practice added energy rather than depleting it, and showing up for myself made it possible to show up for her.

The question works for every kind of low. One thing before the job that's draining you, one thing before the news cycle that's defeating you, one thing before the diagnosis that's terrifying you. That's where it starts.

## Not Everyone Starts From the Same Place

Not everyone can access the tools this particular practice uses equally. The gym membership, the time in the morning before the family wakes up, the physician who speaks plainly, the financial stability that made a health focus possible — pretending everyone can access these equally would be dishonest.

The tools look different for different people. The practice doesn't. The hope practice at its most accessible is the question above: what's one thing you can do today for yourself? The keyword, the receipts, the circle, the Renewal Loop — all of that comes later, if it fits. The one thing comes first.

## Find Your Version

If there is one thing to take from this entire Substack, it's this: be inspired to find a way that suits your circumstances. Not to copy this practice, not to follow these steps, but to find your own version. The practice is real, the principles hold, and agency and pathways — the belief that you can move forward and the discipline to find the route — are available to anyone willing to try.

The inner critic's job is to protect comfort. Your agency over hope lives just outside that comfort zone. You have more options than the news cycle shows you, and more support than a solo effort allows. The posts on this Substack are not a prescription. They are an invitation — the hope practice belongs to anyone willing to show up imperfectly and consistently, in whatever form fits their life and their season.

## The First Step Is Smaller Than You Think

For the person looking for something to grab onto: this is it — not the whole answer, not a guarantee, just a practice honestly described by someone who needed it badly and found that it worked, not perfectly, not always, but consistently enough to matter.

You are not alone in this. There is a way forward, and the first step is smaller than the inner critic's noise makes it seem.

What's one thing you can do today? Write it down. That moment of writing is already the beginning of your hope practice.
