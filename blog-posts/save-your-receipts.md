# Save Your Receipts

### My dad used to say this about money. It turns out it applies to hope too.

My dad was a practical man. He had a phrase he used whenever money was involved.

Save your receipts.

Not because he was suspicious — because he understood that proof matters. Someday, someone will question the transaction. Someday you'll need to show what actually happened. The receipt is the record, and you'll want to have it.

I've been thinking about that advice a lot lately — not about money, but about a hope practice.

The inner critic doesn't just show up at 5 am. It shows up during plateaus. During hard weeks. During the slow middle of a long journey, when the early wins are behind you, and the next milestone isn't visible yet. It shows up with a question: *Is this actually working? Are you sure anything has changed?*

The receipt is your answer. Here's what actually happened. Here's the proof.

## What Gets Measured Gets Real

James Clear makes a precise argument in *Atomic Habits* about the visibility of progress. Small improvements compound over time — 1% better each day adds up to something significant over months and years. To see that, though, you have to be able to see the trend. One data point is noise. A hundred data points is a story.

Tracking isn't just record-keeping. It changes your relationship with the practice. When you can see progress — even slow, directional progress — you stop relying entirely on motivation and mood. The data does some of the carrying. The receipt tells you the truth when the critic is lying.

Clear also makes the point that directional trends matter more than individual data points. A bad week doesn't erase a good month. A plateau doesn't cancel six months of consistent effort. The receipt shows the full picture — not just today, but the whole journey.

## The Dumbbell Ritual

The most visceral receipt I have isn't a number on a screen — it's a dumbbell.

Early in the journey, I started a ritual at the gym. Every time I hit a meaningful milestone, I went to the weight room and picked up the dumbbell corresponding to the weight I had lost. Not as a stunt. As a reckoning.

There is something that happens when you hold that weight in your hands. You feel — physically, in your body — what your joints and your heart were carrying every single moment of every single day. No spreadsheet captures that. No app delivers it. The dumbbell is the receipt you can feel.

At 80 lbs lost, I weighed in after a workout, showered, got ready for the day, and walked back to the weight room. I picked up the 80 lb dumbbell, paused, nodded, and grinned. A private moment — little did I know it wasn't entirely. A longtime friend who understood what this journey meant happened to see it. He told me later what he witnessed. That's a different kind of receipt — being seen by someone who understood the weight of the moment, literally and figuratively.

Save your receipts. Some of them you hold in your hands.

## The Tracking Data

I track body composition — not just weight, but the trend lines that tell a more complete story. Muscle, fat, and the ratio between them. Week over week, the numbers move. Not always in the direction I want. Not always by as much as I'd like. The direction, over time, is the story.

This is Clear's 1% principle in practice. No single workout transforms anything. No single meal matters much in isolation. The consistent, compounding effect of showing up — tracked and visible — tells a different story than any single morning could.

When the critic says nothing is changing, the data says otherwise. That's the receipt.

## The Medical Metrics

This journey started at a doctor's office. A visit that made some things very clear. A list of medications I was none too happy about.

The medical metrics are the receipts I didn't choose to collect — they were collected for me. Blood markers. Lab work. The numbers a physician tracks over time. Those numbers have moved. Not because I wished them to. Because the practice moved them.

Your doctor keeps receipts, too. Ask to see them. They tell a story worth knowing.

## The Smartphone

Here we go with Pictures again. The camera roll doesn't lie.

Two years of photographs tell a story that no single moment can tell. The before isn't always obvious when you're living inside it. The after isn't always obvious either — the changes happen slowly, then all at once. The photographs are dated. The sequence is clear. The receipt is right there in your pocket.

This connects to something Dr. Jane McGonigal documents in her research — that vivid images of your future self make that person feel real rather than abstract. The photograph works in both directions. Looking back at who you were makes the distance you've traveled visible. Looking forward to who you are becoming makes the next step worth taking.

## Reducing Micro-Friction

The friction audit is a receipt, too.

Every gap you've found and closed. Every countermeasure you've built. Every morning, the backup earbuds were in the bag, the clothes were already laid out, the plan for when the tech fails was already in place — those are receipts. Evidence that the practice is being refined, not just repeated.

Showing up is the minimum. Getting smarter about how you show up is the compound interest.

## How AI Can Help

This is a newer receipt in my collection — and one worth naming.

AI tools have become a genuine part of how I reflect on and refine the practice. Not as a replacement for the work, but as a thinking partner for the retrospective. Asking questions that surface patterns. Helping organize tracking data into a readable format. Drafting reflections that make the invisible visible.

The practice generates more data than any one person can easily interpret in the moment. AI helps make sense of it — and that sense-making is itself a form of receipt. Here's what the pattern shows. Here's what the trend suggests. Here's what the retrospective reveals.

## The Retrospective

All of these receipts feed one thing: the retrospective.

Agile Best Self Principle 12 states it directly. At regular intervals, reflect on how to become your best self, then tune and adjust. You cannot tune what you haven't measured. You cannot adjust what you haven't tracked. The retrospective without receipts is just opinion.

With receipts, it becomes something different. Evidence-based reflection. The kind that actually changes what you do next.

Save your receipts. Not because you're suspicious of the practice. Because someday, during a plateau, a hard week, a slow and unglamorous middle, the critic will demand proof.

You'll want to have it.
