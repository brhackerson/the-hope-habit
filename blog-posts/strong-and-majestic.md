# Strong and Majestic
## An Old Story, a New Understanding

*Published: hopeisnotafeeling.substack.com — May 27, 2026*

---

I am not a fisherman. Never have been. Next week, I am fortunate to be able to go back to northern Ontario for the same annual trip with the same group of men from my church. Same lake. A different lodge this time, but the same idea: truly off the grid, away from everything that usually demands attention. That's what prompted me to revisit this story.

In June 2023, we decided to go bigger. A guided trip to Lac Seul, a massive body of water near Sioux Lookout, Ontario, was a significant step up from our usual outings. Our guide was a young guy named Zack, who had the kind of passion for his work that you don't see every day. He was in constant motion for two straight days: navigating the lake, coaching our technique, baiting hooks, landing fish, and cooking shore lunch from scratch over an open fire. Over 200 fish in two days. Jim, who has been a steady presence on these trips, was there. It was the kind of trip that sets the standard for every trip that follows.

But the thing I brought home had nothing to do with walleye.

Zack told us a story about another guide, a man from the Ojibwe nation. One morning on the dock, Zack asked him how he was doing. The man said:

> **"I am strong and majestic."**

That was it. No elaboration. Four words, said with complete conviction, as a full answer to an ordinary question.

I turned that over in my head for the rest of the trip. The more I sat with it, the more it landed as something worth carrying. Not a motivational slogan. Not a performance. A declaration of who you are. A decision about how to show up was made before the day had a chance to tell you otherwise.

I still use it. Almost three years later, in the weight room early in the morning, when I am facing a set that I am not totally sure I can finish, I say it to myself. I am strong and majestic. It focuses on something. The inner critic goes quiet for a moment, which is sometimes all the room you need.

Here is what I now understand about that phrase that I didn't fully have language for in 2023: it is a receipt before the receipt arrives.

The Hope Cycle moves through four stages: build, receive, recognize, and release. Most of the time, we think of receiving hope as something that happens after the work, after the evidence shows up, after the scan comes back with good news, or the mirror finally shows what we have been building. That's one kind of receipt. Another variety arrives before the evidence, in the form of a declaration that reframes who you are in the moment before the outcome is known.

I am strong and majestic, that's the second variety. It doesn't claim the result. It claims the identity: this is who I am as I attempt this thing, regardless of the outcome. The attempt itself is the receipt.

These thoughts connect to something the Agile Best Self mindset has always centered on: intentional design, deciding how you want to arrive before circumstances decide for you. Agile Best Self Principle 12 asks us to regularly reflect on who we are trying to become and then tune our behavior to align with that. A phrase like strong and majestic isn't decoration. It's an alignment tool. It closes the gap between the person you are trying to become and the person who walked into the room.

What I also couldn't have named in 2023 was what made that fishing trip the right container for this kind of insight. I felt rested. I was away from the inbox. I was with people I trusted, doing something simple, on a lake so large it made every other problem feel proportionate. These aren't luxuries. They are the conditions where the Receive step of the Hope Cycle becomes possible. Hope doesn't always arrive as data. Sometimes it arrives as words overheard on a dock, passed from one person to another without anyone intending them as a gift.

The Ojibwe guide didn't know I existed. The gift traveled anyway.

Your version of strong and majestic is yours to find. The specific words matter less than the function they serve: a declaration of identity, stated before the outcome is known, that gives the inner critic something to push against. Something that lands in your spine and across your shoulders when you say it.

You have a keyword in your practice, or something close to one, even if you haven't named it yet. You have a phrase, an image, a sentence that carries more weight than its word count suggests. The hope practice gives you a structure for finding it and using it intentionally, not just when inspiration strikes, but on the ordinary Tuesdays when the set looks heavy, and the inner critic is already making its case.

Say the thing that's true about who you are. Say it before you're sure it's true. That's the Receive step working in reverse. That's hope building itself.

I recognize that a guided fishing trip to northern Ontario is not universally accessible, and the insight I found there was just as available on a morning walk or in a quiet hour at home. The conditions for receiving hope don't require a plane ticket. They require some version of stillness, some company worth trusting, and enough distance from the urgent to let the important surface.

The Ojibwe man on that dock has no idea his words are still moving. That's how hope passes between people: quietly, without ceremony, often without the giver ever knowing the gift landed. You carry phrases you've received from people who never knew they gave them. The question worth sitting with before your next hard moment is: what is yours?
