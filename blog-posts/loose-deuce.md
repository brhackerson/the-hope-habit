# Hope Showed Up Before Anyone Called It That
## Self-discipline meets adversity. That's where hope lives.

---

Hope is what happens when self-discipline meets adversity and wins.

I've said that a lot. I believe it. I also know it can sound like a slogan until you see it happen to an actual group of people in an actual moment of history. The story I want to tell you today is not mine. It belongs to a cadet company at West Point, sixty years ago, and it changed how I think about what hope actually requires.

---

In 1963, there were twenty-four companies at West Point competing on the parade field three times a week. Company L2, the Loose Deuce, had been at the bottom of those rankings for more than a century.[¹²]

Jeff Sutherland, in his last year as a cadet, was appointed training officer of the Loose Deuce. The training officer has no direct power, not part of the command structure, with no one required to do what he says. What Sutherland decided to do with that constraint is the whole story.

He made colored charts. What went right after each parade. What went wrong. No punishments. No blame. Just the facts, posted in the barracks where everyone in the company would see them every day.

Within a few weeks the cadets sharpened their game. A few weeks after that, Company L2 was the number one company at West Point.

Almost a year later, General Douglas MacArthur died. One company was selected to march at his funeral behind the caisson carrying his casket. The Loose Deuce, the same company that had been the worst in the Corps for more than a hundred years, marched that day.

At Sutherland's graduation a few months later, his future father-in-law watched from the crowd. He asked about one company in particular. "They were different than all the rest. The others were marching; they seemed to be floating. Who were they?"

"That was my company," Sutherland told him. "Those men buried General MacArthur."

Sutherland writes that his company had achieved transcendence. That is exactly the right word.

---

Scrum hadn't been invented yet when this happened. Sutherland would spend the next thirty years building the methodology that would change how teams work across every industry in the world. The values he would eventually codify were already running in that barracks in 1963.

The five Scrum values are commitment, courage, focus, openness, and respect. The Loose Deuce story turns on openness. Not openness as a soft word for being nice about feedback. Openness as the discipline of making performance visible without blame, of letting the facts speak without protection or excuse, of trusting that a group of people who can see clearly what's wrong will eventually fix it. That kind of openness requires courage. Maintaining it requires commitment. Doing it in a way that preserves the dignity of the people involved requires respect.

The values don't operate alone. They operate as a system. One supports the next. Sutherland didn't pick one value and hang a poster. He created the conditions where all five could run at once, and then he stood back and let the compound interest accumulate.

Within weeks, a unit that had been failing for a hundred years wasn't failing anymore.

"Scrum doesn't solve your problems," Jeff Sutherland has said. "It just makes them painfully visible." That's not a criticism. That's the mechanism. Visible problems are solvable problems. The Loose Deuce couldn't fix what it couldn't see. Once it could see, everything changed.

---

I came to the Scrum values through Jeff's work, and they became a personal operating system long before they became part of my health journey. When I walked into the gym in February 2025, those values were already running in the background.

Commitment: one day. One session. No grand plan.
Courage: showing up when the inner critic is loudest.
Focus: the keyword, cutting through the noise in under a second.
Openness: the InBody scan, the scale, the data I'd rather not see but need.
Respect: treating myself the way I'd treat someone whose success I genuinely wanted.

The hope practice is the Scrum values applied to the individual. The daily workout is the Daily Scrum: a short, focused commitment, daily accountability, daily inspection and adaptation. The InBody scan is the Sprint Review: concrete evidence of what the practice actually produced. The 1 pm caffeine cutoff is a retrospective finding, a small experiment run, a result confirmed, a behavior changed. Inspect and adapt, one morning at a time.

None of this requires knowing what Scrum is. The values work whether or not you can name them. Sutherland's cadets didn't call it openness. They called it looking at the board.

---

Here's what the Loose Deuce story gives you as a reader, regardless of whether you've ever heard of Scrum.

The company had been failing for more than a century. Not a bad week. Not a rough patch. A hundred years of consistent mediocrity, handed down from one group of cadets to the next like a tradition. The culture said: this is what we are. The data said: this is what we do. The history said: this is how it ends.

And then it didn't.

Not because the adversity went away. The parade field was still there. The competition was still there. The hundred years of losing was still part of the story. What changed was the self-discipline: the commitment to see clearly, the courage to act on what was visible, the focus to improve one specific thing at a time, the openness to let the facts lead, and the respect to hold each other accountable without blame.

That is what the hope practice asks of you. Not perfection. Not a transformation that erases the past. Self-discipline in the face of adversity, sustained long enough for the compound interest to show up in a way that surprises even the people who knew you when you were losing.

Your adversity is not the parade field. Your century of losing looks different from theirs. The values work anyway. The mechanism is the same.

Pick the one that's hardest for you right now.

Commitment, when the alarm goes off and every part of you wants to negotiate. Courage, when the inner critic says not today. Focus, when the noise is louder than the practice. Openness, when the data shows something you'd rather explain away. Respect, for yourself, for the practice, for the person you are trying to become.

One value. One morning. The hope practice starts there.

---

The Loose Deuce went from worst to first to floating above a general's funeral procession. It happened because self-discipline showed up when adversity said there was no reason for it. That is hope, made visible, in the actual world, sixty years ago.

It is still happening. Every morning someone shows up to the practice when they don't feel like it, the Loose Deuce is marching somewhere in the world. Floating when everyone else is just marching.

That's what the hope practice builds. Not a feeling. A team. Even if the team is only you.
