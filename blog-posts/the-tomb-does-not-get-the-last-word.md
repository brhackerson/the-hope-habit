# The Tomb Does Not Get the Last Word

### If you want the ripple to travel, you have to put something in the water.

Easter weekend, April 2026.

Two things arrived over the course of the weekend, from two different people, from far away. Both found their way to me in Minnesota through the community that has been forming around this work.

The first came on Saturday from my sister Sandra, who was traveling in Colorado. A photograph of a mural on a brick wall, painted with a large flower, and three lines of text:

*Joy is resistance.* *Hope is discipline.* *Love is courage.*

The second came on Easter Sunday, a link from my sister-in-law Marsha, who has been reading the Substack. A one-minute CNN interview with Senator Raphael Warnock, speaking with Jake Tapper ahead of his Easter sermon at Ebenezer Baptist Church in Atlanta. In this church, Martin Luther King Jr. once stood.

Neither Sandra nor Marsha was looking for it. Both of them found something that pointed directly at the work.

That is the community doing what communities do.

## What Warnock Said

Senator Warnock's message was short and precise. He said he doesn't like to talk much about optimism. Too milquetoast. Optimism, he said, denies the tragedies of the human experience.

Hope is different.

Hope recognizes there is tragedy. A Crucifixion. A tomb. Hope doesn't float above the hard thing. It walks directly into it and says, "This is real, and it is not the end of the story."

The Resurrection, he said, reminds us that the tomb does not get the last word. There can be new human possibility.

I have been writing about the distinction between hope and optimism since February. I have been saying that optimism is a disposition, warm and useful, but ultimately passive. That hope is different because it meets adversity rather than rising above it; the hope that holds is forged in the hard things, not despite them.

On Easter Sunday, in a CNN interview, a senator said the same thing in fewer words than I have used in six posts.

The tomb does not get the last word.

## What This Means for the "Win"

I have been careful about the word "win" in the central expression of this practice: hope is what happens when self-discipline meets adversity and wins.

The win is not the absence of the tomb. The win is that the tomb does not get the last word.

For someone on a recoverable journey, the win looks like forward momentum. For someone in an impossible situation, the win looks like the story continuing: one more day of presence, one more act of meaning, one more moment of choosing something other than despair. The Resurrection doesn't erase the Crucifixion. It says the Crucifixion was not the end.

That is wide enough for everyone.

## How They Found This

Sandra found the mural and sent it. Marsha found the video previewing the sermon and sent it to me. Neither was searching for something to send me. Both were simply in community with the work, paying attention, pointing in the same direction, and something found them.

That is what I think of as second-level impact. The practice traveled through the community, changed something about how both Sandra and Marsha were paying attention, and then came back in ways I hadn't expected and couldn't have engineered.

You don't get to watch the ripple. You don't know who is reading while working through something difficult. You don't know whose sister will find the mural, whose sister-in-law will find an impactful video, whose daughter has the monkey. The hope you release into a community doesn't need a receipt. It just needs to be released.

## The Second Level

The hope you release into a community travels in directions you don't choose and reaches people you don't know.

The mural traveled from a wall to a phone to a text message to this post. The sermon traveled from Ebenezer Baptist Church to CNN to a sister-in-law to here. Neither stopped where it started. Both are still moving.

That is how communities carry hope further than any individual could carry it alone. The contribution doesn't get divided among the people who share it. It multiplies. Every person who passes something forward adds force to it.

## What This Asks of You

If you want the ripple to travel, you have to put something in the water.

A post. A conversation. A shared value. A phone call to someone who needed it. A presence in a room where hope was running low. An act of showing up was the most hope you had to offer.

Communities amplify what individuals release. The We Power that the research confirmed means the hope that lives in relationship travels further and holds longer than the hope that lives in isolation.

Your job is not to track where it goes. Your job is to release it.

## The Practice and the Resurrection

I am not a theologian. I hold my faith with humility and uncertainty, the way I hold most things that matter.

But I know what it means to sit in a room in December, watching someone I loved move toward a closing, and feel the weight of a story that seemed to be ending. And I know what it meant, weeks later, in a communion line on Epiphany Sunday, to receive a paper star with a single word on it.

The practice that followed was not due to hope arriving. It was because I decided to build it: one boring win at a time, through the long and unglamorous middle, with the help of a circle I didn't fully know was there.

The Resurrection is the original hope practice. The tomb does not get the last word. Neither does the hard thing you're facing. Build the practice. Put something in the water.
