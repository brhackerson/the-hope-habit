# When Hope Finds You

### The practice doesn't just build hope. It tunes the antenna to receive it.

---

Most writing about hope focuses on building it — the keyword, the habits, the early mornings, the receipts. That work is real and necessary. But there is another side to the practice that doesn't get named often enough: the receiving end.

A well-built hope practice doesn't just generate hope from within. It changes how you move through the world. It tunes something in you so that when hope arrives from outside — through a person, a moment, an unexpected gift — you actually notice it. You let it land. You don't explain it away or move past it too quickly.

That is a practice too.

## Where Hope Lives in the Body

The best way I can describe what hope feels like physically is this: it arrives in the face first. Something releases in the jaw, around the eyes, in the muscles that have been holding tension without announcing it. Sometimes it forces a smile. Sometimes it just softens what was tight. From there it travels — a feeling of lightness that moves through the rest of the body, not euphoria, just a quiet lifting of something that had been pressing down.

The clearest example I have is a specific day last year. It was my first strength training session after surgery — the body showing up, proving it could still do this. That same day, a friend who had been following the journey sent a photograph. He had seen what the work meant, and he wanted me to know he had seen it.

Two receipts arriving at once. One from the body, one from the circle. Hope showed up in my face that day and didn't leave for a while.

## The Practice as a Receiver

Here is what surprised me most about building a hope practice: it didn't just make me better at generating hope. It made me better at receiving it.

The star word is the clearest example. On Epiphany Sunday 2025, about a week after my mother passed, I was in a communion line at church. A stranger handed me a paper star. On it was a single word: hope.

I returned to my pew and the tears came before anything else. It had only been a week. I was still wobbly. But after a moment I found the words to say to Debby: how amazing it is sometimes when what you need finds you in a way you don't expect.

That star went up on the wall by my gym bag the same day. It is still there, now surrounded by Post-its — insights from others that arrived over time. Dr. Raphael Warnock. Pete Buttigieg. Others. The wall became a small, private record of hope received.

The practice was already underway when that star arrived. I believe that is why it landed the way it did. The antenna was tuned. The window was open.

## Gratitude as the Discipline of Receiving

Dr. Robert Emmons and Dr. Michael McCullough, whose landmark 2003 research established much of what we know about gratitude, define it as a two-step process: recognizing that something good arrived, and recognizing that it came from an external source. That second step is the one most people skip. Something good happens and we attribute it to luck, or timing, or coincidence. We don't fully receive it because we don't fully name it.

Gratitude is the practice of naming what arrived and where it came from. It is the discipline of letting hope land rather than moving past it. The star word didn't just happen to arrive. It arrived through a community pointed in the same direction, through a ritual that created the conditions for unexpected gifts. Naming that — sitting with it, taping it to the wall — is what made it real.

This is not a passive practice. It requires the same intentionality as the 5am alarm.

## Spreading It Outward

Once hope has been received and recognized, the next act is release. Intentionality matters most here.

Spreading hope is not performing positivity. It is not announcing that things will work out or pretending the hard parts aren't real. People can tell the difference between optimism performed and hope that comes from a real practice. The latter has weight. It has evidence underneath it. It has receipts.

Dr. Brené Brown's definition of vulnerability is the courage to show up and be seen without guarantee of outcome. That is exactly what releasing hope into the world requires. You don't know who is watching. You cannot predict where the ripple travels. You have to get into position to be seen and then trust the community to carry what you put in the water.

The Substack post published late on a Saturday. Sandra in Colorado finding a mural that said exactly what the practice had been trying to say. Marsha sending a one-minute video from a CNN interview that resolved a question the book had been wrestling with for months. None of these were planned. All of them required someone to put something in the water first.

Small moments carry it further than any grand gesture. A post. A conversation. A phone call to someone who needed it. The act of showing up consistently, in communities pointed in the same direction, is how hope travels.

## The Renewal Loop

Here is the part that took me the longest to understand: giving hope outward does not deplete the supply. It replenishes it.

When the community series concluded and the responses started arriving — Sandra's mural, Marsha's video, messages from readers applying the practice to their own journeys — something was restored that the writing had drawn from. The contribution fueled the recovery. The giving made room for more.

Dr. Gervais talks about seasons of emphasis rather than an off-season. Contribution and recovery are not opposites in the hope practice. They are partners. The more you release with intention, the more you have to receive. The loop is self-sustaining when both sides are active.

This is what the research from Colla, Williams, Oades, and Camacho-Morles called We Power — the interpersonal dimension of hope that sustains itself through community. You are not the only source. The community carries it. Your job is to keep contributing and keep receiving, and to recognize that both are part of the same practice.

## The Practice Is a Full Circle

Build it. Receive it. Recognize it. Release it.

The star word on the wall by the gym bag is the practice in miniature. Something arrived from outside. It was received in tears and recognized as a gift. It was taped to the wall where the practice begins every morning. From there it has been released outward — in posts, in conversations, in a book taking shape — to people who are looking for exactly what that word contains.

Hope finds you when the practice is underway. The antenna is tuned. The window is open. What arrives through it is worth receiving fully, naming honestly, and releasing with intention.

Put something in the water. Then watch what comes back.
