# Hope Doesn't Only Live in the Future

### My inner critic argues with everything. Except the evidence.

---

At my mother's visitation, a photo loop played on a screen near the entrance.

Family photographs. Decades of them. Mom at different ages, different seasons, different rooms. The day was full and busy, greeting family and friends who had come, being present for others, carrying the moment the way you do when community gathers around loss. She had passed four months earlier, and the sharpest grief had already done much of its work.

At some point, I walked past the screen and caught a few photographs of myself in the loop, almost in passing. An older, heavier version, standing in gatherings and celebrations that I remember well. I didn't stop. The day had too much else happening.

Something registered quietly as I moved around the room. The distance between the person on that screen and the person walking through that room was real. The old photographs were evidence: not of failure, but of distance traveled. In that quiet recognition, something that felt like hope arrived, not from imagining the future, but from seeing what the practice had already built.

That is what this post is about.

## Works in Both Directions

In an earlier article, I wrote about the concept of a keyword: a single word that serves as an instant gateway to your deepest why, accessible in the hardest moments. Mine is Pictures. The future photographs I plan to be in with the people I love most, the grandchildren who don't exist yet, the graduation, at memorable golf courses, the harness closing with room to spare on a theme park ride that didn't close the last time, those are what get me out of bed at 5 am on the hard mornings.

Old photographs work from the other side, as I have come to appreciate. They show where you were. They hold the starting point that makes the destination meaningful. Unlike the inner critic, which rewrites the past constantly in its own favor, the camera never lies.

The keyword isn't just a forward-looking practice. It works in both directions simultaneously. Most people only use one.

## The Data Version

The InBody scan history is one of the clearest old receipts in the practice. An InBody machine measures body composition, including weight, skeletal muscle mass, body fat percentage, and hydration levels, by sending a small, painless electrical signal through your body. Different tissues conduct electricity differently, which allows the machine to estimate how your body breaks down into muscle, fat, and water. It is not a clinically precise instrument; a more sophisticated process would be required to achieve that level of accuracy. The direction of the trends over time is meaningful data. Not any single number, but the direction the numbers are moving. Life Time, where I train, provides access to one.

The numbers from before the practice began tell a story the inner critic would rather rewrite. Looking back at them from where I stand now produces one feeling above all others: pride. And one question keeps coming back: how did I carry all that around all day?

More old receipts: Down nearly 90 lbs. Body fat has been cut nearly in half. Skeletal muscle mass has been maintained throughout the transformation and rebuilt after surgery. Those are not small movements. That is a different body living a different life.

The dumbbell ritual is the physical version of this same receipt. Years ago, I developed a tradition: when I reached a milestone in weight lost, I would pick up the dumbbell matching that number, not to lift it as exercise, but to feel what I had been carrying. That attempt didn't hold. When this practice took root in 2025, the ritual came back with it. The 80 lb dumbbell says: this weight moved through every step and every stair of every ordinary day. Holding it now, one hand, easy, is a receipt you can feel.

## The Inner Critic vs. The Evidence

The inner critic uses old receipts, too. It keeps them close and quotes them selectively.

On the two-hour drive home from dropping Jacob off at college in February 2025, as I sorted out what to do next to become someone different, the inner critic was already filing evidence: you've tried before, and it didn't hold; here's the pattern, here's the proof. All of it was true. All of it was very, very real.

The practice reads the same receipts differently.

Here's where you were. Here's where you are. The distance in between is the receipt. The attempts that didn't hold weren't failures; they were the practice finding its form, the season not yet ready, the pile not yet heavy enough to produce the decision that would finally hold.

There's a quote often attributed to John Shedd: "Ships are safe in harbor, but that's not what ships are built for." The harbor is the comfort zone: the place that feels safe and familiar, but keeps you from what you were built to do.

The harbor concept captures this precisely in the context of old receipts. The person who stayed in the harbor was reading old receipts as evidence that the sea was too dangerous. The person who left the dock was reading the same receipts as evidence of what was possible. Same data. Different reading. Everything different.

The inner critic argues with everything. It debates the morning, disputes the progress, and rewrites the past to serve its narrative. There is one thing it cannot argue with: the old photograph. The number on the old scan. The dumbbell in the hand.

The evidence was always there. The inner critic just hoped you wouldn't look.

## Borrowed Hope From the Past Self

Here is the part that took me the longest to name.

The person in the old photograph, heavier and tired, before the practice began, was already doing something remarkable. That person was carrying everything forward. Getting up. Showing up. Loving his family. Playing golf when his body made it hard.

That person was already building something, even without knowing the name. Self-discipline meets the quiet adversity of an ordinary life and shows up anyway. That is the hope practice before it had a name.

Looking back at that person is not pity. It is receiving hope from someone who had less reason to hope than you do now. The past self is a source of hope for the present self. The forward-looking version of Pictures doesn't account for that. It is real, and it matters on the mornings when the present self needs reminding that it has already proven something.

Your keyword might not be Pictures. Your old receipts might not be body scans or dumbbells. The practice looks different for everyone; it might be financial, relational, creative, or something else entirely. I also recognize that not everyone has the time, the gym membership, the resources, or the support that made this practice possible to the same extent. The tools look different for different people. The practice doesn't. The structure is the same: somewhere behind you, a person was already doing the work. Getting up. Showing up. Keeping going before they knew what they were building. That person is a source of hope. Look back and receive it.

## The Generational Receipt

There is a photograph of my father in his Air Force dress khaki uniform, lean and sharp, receiving a commendation from his commanding officer. My mother stands beside him, healthy and beautiful. A very young version of me, maybe three years old, is in the photograph too. Mom was about a year past delivering the twins.

The feeling when I found that photograph: pride. Not grief. Pride.

He was that person: accomplished, serving his country, standing beside the people he loved before the road took so much of him. Before the years of giving everything outward. Before the long decline.

*[Photo: Dad in his Air Force dress khaki uniform, with Mom and a young Brian, late 1960s.]*

That photograph holds the best of who he was. My hope practice is being built in his honor, taking what was awesome and choosing different paths for the rest. I look back at that photograph and find hope there, too. Not in what was lost. In what was real. He was that person. The photograph proves it.

The keyword traces back to that photograph. To that uniform. To that young family standing together in the late 1960s.

## Both Directions

Old pictures. Old receipts. Both directions.

The keyword that pulls you toward the grandchildren who don't exist yet also reaches back to the father in his dress khakis and the old scan data and the brief glimpse at a photo loop on a busy day.

The inner critic argues with everything. It disputes the present, rewrites the past, and works to keep you in harbor.

Except the evidence. The inner critic has no answer for the old photograph. It has no counter to the numbers on the old scan. It cannot rewrite what the camera already captured.

Look back. The evidence was always there. The practice was always building something. Hope doesn't only live in the future. It lives in the distance already traveled, in the proof already gathered, in the person who kept going before the practice had a name.

The future is where hope points. The past is where it already proved itself.
