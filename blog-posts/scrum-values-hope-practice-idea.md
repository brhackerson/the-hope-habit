## The Core Argument

Most Scrum practitioners know the five values as a team operating system. This post makes the case that they are also a personal hope practice — and always were.

---

## Historical Hook

The Scrum Values were not part of Scrum from the beginning. Sutherland and Schwaber added them in the 2016 revision of the Scrum Guide. The implication: the mechanics of Scrum alone were not enough. The human dimension had to be named explicitly.

Hope practice works the same way. The habits, the keyword, the daily routine are the mechanics. The values — courage, commitment, focus, openness, respect — are what make the practice sustainable at a human level.

---

## High-Level Outline

**Opening** — Most Scrum practitioners know the five values as a team operating system. This post makes the case that they are also a personal hope practice — and always were. The historical hook: the Values were added in 2016 because mechanics alone weren't enough.

**Courage** — Hope requires meeting adversity, not avoiding it. Courage is what gets you to the meeting. Without it, self-discipline never starts.

**Commitment** — This is the self-discipline engine. Showing up consistently for what matters, especially when you don't feel like it. Loyalty to your future self expressed as daily action.

**Focus** — The keyword. The North Star. The single thing that cuts through noise at 5am when the critic is loudest. Focus is what makes hope actionable in the hardest moment.

**Openness** — Hope that cannot adapt does not survive adversity. Openness is Principle 12 in action — inspect, adjust, stay aligned. The approach evolves; the intention holds.

**Respect** — The one most people miss. You have to believe the practice is worth doing for yourself. Respect starts with you. A hope practice built on self-contempt collapses.

**Closing** — The five values work together as a system, in Scrum and in life. You do not pick two and skip three. The practice holds because all five hold it.
