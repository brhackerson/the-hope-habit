# The Inner Critic Works Alone. I Don't.

### The inner advocate doesn't show up on its own. It gets built by the people around you.

In March or April of 2025, a few months into the journey, I hit a wall I wasn't expecting.

The scale was moving in the right direction. But my lean muscle mass was moving the wrong way. Body weight down. Lean muscle mass was headed down, too. The inner critic, which had been relatively quiet while the numbers were moving, suddenly had something to say. *See? You're losing the wrong thing. This isn't working the way you think it is.*

I didn't have an answer for that. Not yet.

So I did something simple. I walked over to one of the trainers at Life Time and asked a question. No appointment. No fee. Just a question.

What he told me stopped me cold. At over 50, the body loses lean muscle mass quickly, faster than most people realize. Creatine can help reverse that curve. He said it matter-of-factly, like it was something everyone over 50 should know.

I was skeptical. Creatine felt like something for bodybuilders, not for a 60-year-old on a health journey. So I took it straight to my doctor: Is creatine something that could safely help me?

The response came fast. A resounding yes.

Within a month, the lean muscle mass curve started bending the other way. Body weight continued to drop at the same pace. Two curves are now moving in the right direction.

That was the moment the inner advocate started showing up regularly. The inner critic took a back seat. Not gone, but quieter. Less in charge. The circle had delivered the answer. The data confirmed it. The practice deepened.

I did not figure that out alone.

## The Inner Advocate Needs Fuel

The inner critic is self-sustaining. It doesn't need outside help. Doubt, fatigue, a bad week, a plateau, a dead earbud. It finds fuel everywhere.

The inner advocate is different. It runs on evidence. On encouragement. On knowledge it didn't have before. On being seen by someone who understands what the journey costs. It needs the circle to keep it fed.

Agile Best Self Principle 4 centers on this idea. Your trusted circle is not optional. I think of it as critical infrastructure. The people around you either add fuel to the practice or take it. The ones who hold hope for you are the ones who keep the inner advocate in the room.

My circle turned out to be wider than I realized. Some of its members didn't even know they were in it.

## The People Who Taught Me

Some of the most important teaching happened years before 2025.

When I worked at 3M, I had access to a corporate gym and collaborated with the trainers there: Chris, Anna, Sid, and Darcy. They taught me how to build a full-body strength plan. I didn't know then that I was building a foundation I would return to a decade later. That knowledge was sitting there, waiting. When the 2025 journey started, I wasn't starting from zero. I was starting from something.

My physician, Dr. Lee, has been more than the person who delivered the news that inspired me to start the journey. He gave specific advice on cardio workout strategies: focus on interval training over straight cardio sessions. The heart rate going up and coming back down is good for the heart, he said. He also advised on supplements. Practical, specific guidance that shaped the practice from the start.

The Life Time team filled in some gaps that the doctor and my prior training experiences hadn't covered. Chad and Kaylee offered advice on supplement strategy and suggested exercises, including a conversation about creatine that changed the trajectory of my journey. No formal engagement. No expensive training sessions. Just smart use of what was available.

These are people who taught me. Some long before the journey began.

## The Foundation That Came First

Some of what I leveraged in 2025 was taught to me forty years earlier.

On July 3, 1985, my dad sat down and typed a letter to his children. He called it "Things I Have Learned (Sometimes at Great Expense) Over the Past 22 Plus Years of Married Life." Eleven lessons. Signed, Love, Dad.

Number seven has stayed with me ever since.

*"Prepare yourself for new challenges. I can't remember how many times I've agonized the night before an important exam, meeting, or decision. As long as I was prepared, the next day usually went like a breeze. Prepare yourself for the challenge, and it will go much easier."*

He was talking about exams and meetings. But the principle applies to every 5 am alarm, every gym bag packed the night before, every backup plan built for when the tech fails. Preparation is not anxiety. It is respect for the challenge ahead. It is the decision, made in advance, to give yourself every possible advantage.

And it applies to "Pictures" too — not just as a keyword for the hard morning, but as the whole reason to prepare in the first place. You prepare yourself for the great things still coming. The graduations. The weddings. The grandchildren. The rounds of golf. The moments worth showing up for.

Dad taught me that in 1985. I was applying it in 2025 without realizing where it came from.

He also closed the letter with two lines that tell you everything about who he was: "A wet bird never flies at night" and "A pregnant squirrel seldom climbs." I have no idea what either one means.

## The People Who Cheered and Challenged

My wife Debby has been in it from the beginning. I am grateful for her support, even when the history of past attempts gave her every reason to doubt. She has watched the practice take hold in ways neither of us expected.

My faith community holds a kind of hope I don't always name out loud. The Epiphany Sunday star word, HOPE, was handed to me randomly in the communion line a week after my mom passed. It came from that community. They didn't know what they were giving me. That's the point.

I am fortunate to have a group of friends in my close circle. People I can speak openly with about the hard days and celebrate the real wins with. A safe space is not a luxury. It is where the inner advocate learns to trust itself.

Michaele Gardner has been my thought partner in the Agile Best Self mindset for years. She is a connector and catalyst: people to ideas, ideas to people, people to each other. The principles we built together were already in place. The mindset was the scaffolding. When my journey started in 2025, it was already there, built through years of work alongside her.

## The People Who Supported Without Knowing It

My family, Debby, and our kids are the reason behind my keyword "Pictures." They don't know that on the hardest mornings, their futures are the thing that gets me out of bed. They are stakeholders in the practice without having signed up for it.

My mom, Susan Hackerson, was in the circle in a way that only became clear after she was gone. The kindness she practiced for decades, the monkeys she made, the word that arrived through a stranger in a communion line a week after she passed. She was part of the circle. She always had been.

And my friend Jim, the one who sent the photograph at exactly the right moment after surgery, without knowing what I was feeling. He was just sharing a memory. But the inner advocate recognized it as something more.

## The Tools That Help

AI has become a genuine part of the practice. A thinking partner for the retrospective, a way to surface patterns in the data, a space to make the invisible visible. The food tracking app keeps the receipts honest. The body composition scans tell the directional story.

These are not replacements for the human circle. They are extensions of it. Tools that help the inner advocate do its job when the humans aren't in the room.

## What the Circle Does

The inner critic doesn't need much. It is self-sufficient and resourceful. One bad data point is enough.

The inner advocate needs more. It needs a trainer who knows something you don't. A doctor who says yes, quickly, to a question that matters. A friend who sends a photograph. A community that hands you a word you didn't choose. A thought partner who helped build the mindset before you knew you'd need it. A family whose futures are worth every 5 am alarm.

Build the circle deliberately. Use what it offers. Feed the inner advocate everything the circle gives you.

You can't build your practice alone. You were never supposed to.
