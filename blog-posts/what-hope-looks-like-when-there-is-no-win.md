# What Hope Looks Like When There Is No Win

### My mom's last month taught me something the practice needed.

These posts have been about building a hope practice. A keyword. A friction audit. A daily decision to show up. The practice has been working — and I've been writing about it with a sense of forward momentum, because that's what the journey has felt like.

Then came December 2025.

As I've been sharing this hope practice publicly, a few friends offered perspectives that stopped me — and made me realize the practice needed to be wider than the story I had been telling. Not because anything was wrong with the practice. Because someone I loved was in a room where none of those words applied.

If hope can't hold that room too, it isn't wide enough.

## Who She Was

My mom's name was Susan Hackerson.

She was a former elementary school teacher, later a librarian at Owatonna High School. In her later years, she became a proficient knitter and embroidery maven — annual entries at the Steele County Fair, and a steady, quiet practice of giving her work away. Knitted monkeys. Bunnies. Embroidered dish towels. She donated 12-15 knitted animals each month to Community Pathways in Owatonna, where they were given to people in need of a kindness boost. Our family estimates she gifted over 500 monkeys and bunnies over the years, not counting the dish towels.

She always accompanied the gift with a bow of her head and "thank you for your kindness."

My sister Sandra described her as the Ambassador for Kindness in Owatonna. That's exactly right.

Kindness was not a strategy for her. It was a way of life. She didn't calculate the ripple effect. She just kept making the monkeys. We once heard a story about an EMT who had transported her, who told her that his daughter had one of those animals, and had no idea where it came from. That's what happens when you release kindness into the world and don't keep track of it. It finds people you'll never meet.

## December 2025

When I visited the assisted living facility in her last month, the TV was usually on. She'd be lying in her bed or sitting in her chair. Those visits had a rhythm — conversation, TV, monkeys on the table. Life was still present.

December 2025 was different.

I didn't try to insert hope into those visits. It would have felt hollow. Maybe worse than hollow. She was in pain. The hospice team was professional and caring, focused entirely on her comfort — doing exactly what hospice professionals do. But the pain was real, and I could see it. Hope as a concept had no place in that room. I just showed up and sat with her.

That was enough. That was all there was to do.

## The Day of the House Closing

In those last weeks, one of the things weighing on her was the house. The family had been working hard to get it sold. She was very concerned about it — a stress she carried even as everything else was slipping away.

The day of the closing, I stopped to see her.

I found her lying on her back, covered with a blanket, mouth slightly open. The TV was off. The room was quiet. Very quiet. Very different from how it had been. She could barely comprehend that I was there. She was still able to greet me by name.

She knew who I was. She was still in there.

I told her what I had seen at the house closing meeting. The young buyers with that energy in their eyes — it was their first home — and I connected with what that felt like. The wife's first thought when they got the keys was to set up the Christmas tree. The husband told me it was the only house they had really wanted. They had seen many others. This was the one.

When I finished the story, she smiled. That was all she could give me in that moment.

It was the last smile I would see from her.

A tear leaked from her left eye. I knew she had heard me. A burden she had been carrying — she could let it go now.

## What That Moment Taught Me

That smile was not a win in any conventional sense. She wasn't moving forward. She wasn't overcoming adversity. She wasn't building toward anything.

But something was released. A stress lifted. Peace arrived, albeit briefly and quietly, through a story about a young family's joy in her home. Her home. The place she had made for decades.

That is still hope. It just looks completely different.

Hope in an impossible situation is not forward momentum. It is not winning. Sometimes it is a burden set down. Sometimes it is being present long enough to deliver a story that lets someone breathe easier. Sometimes the person practicing hope is the one in the chair — and sometimes it is the one standing beside it.

The hope practice I have been building is real, and it has changed my life. But it was designed for a journey with a horizon. My mom's last month had no horizon. And hope was still there — quieter, slower, different in form. Not the hope of agency and pathways. The hope of presence. The hope of not being alone. The hope of a young family setting up a Christmas tree in the house you built your life in for four decades.

That is wide enough. That has to be wide enough.

## The Word That Came After

About a week after she passed, I was in the communion line on Epiphany Sunday. The tradition at our church involves handing out star words — a single word printed on a paper star, given randomly to each person. You sit with it for the year. You notice where it shows up, and it showed up in a big way that day.

My word was HOPE.

I didn't choose it. It was random. My family members received other words.

I have that star. I kept it. It sits as a reminder that the word arrived after the practice had already begun — not as a spark, but as a name. She didn't give me the word directly. But I have a hard time believing she had nothing to do with it.

## For the People in That Room

If you are reading this from a room like December — a room where hope feels hollow, where the forward momentum of a practice has no place, where the most honest thing you can do is sit with someone and be present — this practice is for you too.

The win looks different in every situation. Sometimes it is a smile. Sometimes it is a tear. Sometimes it is a burden set down by someone who can barely speak.

For those of faith — and I count myself among them — there is another answer to the question of the win. She is no longer in pain. She is with my dad and with loved ones who went before her. That is a win. A different kind, on a different timeline, but real.
