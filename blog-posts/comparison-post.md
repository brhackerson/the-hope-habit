# Comparison Didn't Steal My Joy. It Built It.
## Hope knows which direction to look.

*Published: hopeisnotafeeling.substack.com — July 10, 2026*

---

A quote widely attributed to Theodore Roosevelt circulates so often it barely registers anymore. "Comparison is the thief of joy." You've seen it on mugs, in Instagram captions, stitched on pillows. It circulates because it's mostly right.

I heard it again recently. Someone shared it in a context that made perfect sense, and for a moment I nodded along, as many do. Then a thought arrived: what does this actually mean for my hope practice?

The part the quote gets wrong is the part that matters most.

Where the quote earns its reputation: comparison with other people, you look at someone else's result and hold it up against your own process. Their finished product against your messy middle. Their highlight reel against your behind-the-scenes footage. My inner critic, the internal voice that defaults to comfort and safety above growth, takes that comparison and runs with it, because it finds ammunition it couldn't generate on its own. You're measuring yourself against someone else's circumstances, advantages, starting points, and choices, none of which you control and none of which have anything to say about your trajectory.

That comparison does steal joy. It also steals presence. The moment you start measuring yourself against someone else, you leave the moment you're actually living. You stop receiving what arrived and start comparing its value to what someone else received. Authenticity collapses immediately, because you're not actually there anymore.

Roosevelt was right about that version. It's a thief. Don't feed it.

---

Here's the version he missed.

On a fishing trip this past June, I came off the lake and found some photos that caused me to pause and take note. I was in the same pose I've had for three years: standing in the boat, holding a walleye with a big smile. I pulled up the same pose from two and three years prior.

The contrast was striking. That comparison didn't steal joy. It produced pride, and it is exactly the kind of comparison my hope practice runs on.

For me, the InBody scan history does the same work. An InBody scan measures body composition over time: muscle mass, body fat, the numbers that tell the real story the scale cannot deliver by itself. The approach of not comparing my body fat percentage to anyone else's, but to what it was six months ago, a year ago, and at the start date of building my hope practice. That comparison tells me something useful, which is whether what I am doing is working. My inner critic cannot argue with three years of the same pose, side by side.

Comparison aimed at your former self is not a thief. It's a receipt.

---

The Recognize step of the Hope Cycle is where this distinction lives. The Hope Cycle (Build, Receive, Recognize, Release) is the engine at the center of my hope practice. The Recognize step is where gratitude does its work: naming what arrived and identifying its source. When you compare yourself to your former self honestly, that's exactly what you're doing: naming the distance covered, receiving what my hope practice built, recognizing the receipt before my inner critic can explain it away.

The inner advocate, the counter-voice that reads the same evidence and reaches a different conclusion, uses comparison with the former self as evidence. My inner critic uses comparison with others as a tactic to move me to safety in my comfort zone. It is the same word, in the opposite direction, delivering an entirely different outcome for my hope practice.

Presence is what separates the two. Comparison with others takes you out of the present moment. It sends you somewhere else, into someone else's story, measuring yourself against a standard you had no hand in setting. Comparison with past versions of yourself does the opposite. It roots you in the present by showing you how far you've traveled to arrive here. You see the moment more clearly, not less. You receive it more fully.

That's not theft. That's the Recognize step working exactly as intended.

---

Your former self is the only fair benchmark. Not the person on the treadmill next to you. Not the success story in the newsletter. Not the before-and-after photo of someone whose starting point you don't know and whose circumstances you can't see. The version of you that started the hope practice, or the version that wanted to start and hadn't found the way yet.

For some people, that backward comparison shows growth. Something changed, the habit took hold, the distance accumulated. The receipt is visible, and the feelings are real.

For others, looking back shows something harder. A body that has declined. A season of loss. A version of the self with greater capacity than the present one. Both comparisons tell the truth about something real. The truth from both has something worth receiving.

The question for the first person: how far have I come? The question for the second: how did I show up? That second question is always answerable, regardless of what the body did or didn't do. The person who stayed present for the people they loved, who kept building whatever version of the hope practice was available, who chose not to retreat when retreating would have been understandable, that comparison produces something worth receiving too. The receipt looks different. The mechanism is the same.

Aim it honestly. The direction is everything.

---

Roosevelt was almost right. Comparison aimed sideways is a thief. Comparison aimed backward builds something the thief can't touch.

The thief can't steal what you've already built.
