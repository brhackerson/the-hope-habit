Self-discipline with the Scrum framework, many examples - when we talk about self-discipline with the Scrum framework, we can talk about the various events and practices that are part of the Scrum framework, within the context of the team, such as:
Daily Scrum: daily commitment to the team, daily accountability, daily inspection and adaptation
Sprint Retrospective: regular commitment to continuous improvement, regular accountability to the team, regular inspection and adaptation of the process, focusing on the most important changes to make to improve the team's performance, which could include stopping practices that are not working, starting new practices that could work, or changing existing practices to make them more effective
Sprint Planning: regular commitment to planning and prioritization, regular accountability to the team, regular inspection and adaptation of the work to be done, focusing on the most important work to be done in the next sprint
Sprint Review: regular commitment to transparency and feedback, regular accountability to stakeholders, regular inspection and adaptation of the product, focusing on the most important feedback to be received and acted upon
Values-based Scrum self-discipline: all team members commit to the Scrum values of commitment, courage, focus, openness, and respect, which creates a culture of self-discipline that can help the team overcome adversity and achieve their goals

Hope idea: when a team has self-discipline with the Scrum framework and values, they can create hope even in the face of adversity. The team can see that they have the power to improve their situation through their own actions, which can create a sense of hope and optimism for the future. This is because they know that they have the tools and mindset to overcome challenges and achieve their goals, which can give them a sense of agency and control over their work and their lives.

Example is Jeff Sutherland's story about the Loose Deuce - find it in the red book - Scrum had not been invented yet - when he was in the Academy, he was put in charge of a group of cadets who were considered the worst in the class, and they were called the "Loose Deuce". This unit had a history of poor performance on the marching field, and they were often the last to be chosen for any team or activity. However, Jeff Sutherland decided to take a different approach with this group. Focusing on what is now the Scrum value of openness, he started by creating feedback loops for each team member showing their mistakes and suggestions for improvement.  These were posted on a bulletin board for everyone to see. Many times the board was thrown out of the barracks by the cadets, but Sutherland would put it back up, and eventually, the cadets started to take ownership of their performance and improve. They went from being the worst unit to being one of the best, and they even won a competition against other units. This story illustrates how self-discipline with feedback loops and openness can create hope and lead to success even in the face of adversity.  Read more about this story in the book "Scrum: The Art of Doing Twice the Work in Half the Time" by Jeff Sutherland to verify the details. The feedback loop and the visibility of the feedback created a culture of accountability and continuous improvement, which helped the Loose Deuce to improve their performance and create hope for success. This story is a powerful example of how self-discipline with the Scrum values and framework can create hope and lead to success even in challenging circumstances.

the takeaway is that leaning into the Scrum values can create self-discipline for teams, and for one's self. This self-discipline can create hope for teams and individuals, even in the face of adversity. By committing to the Scrum values and framework, teams can create a culture of continuous improvement and resilience that can help them overcome challenges and achieve their goals.
