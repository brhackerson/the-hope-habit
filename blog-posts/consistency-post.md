# Consistency Doesn't Look Like a Superpower. That's the Point.

### Show up imperfect. Show up anyway.

---

One moment since I started this journey early in 2025 has stayed with me to this date.

I had just started an exercise on a leg press machine. I pushed. Nothing happened. The body simply refused the weight. Not a twinge or a warning. Just a flat refusal. The legs said no.

I reduced the weight significantly and finished the workout.

That is not the story of a superpower. It looks like a setback. It looks like someone having a bad day at the gym. What it actually was: consistency doing exactly what consistency does. Not performing at its best, just showing up and finding a way to continue.

Did I feel disappointed? Yes. Was I going to call it done? Hell no.

That distinction is the whole post.

## Consistency Is Not Perfection

Most people wait for the conditions to be right before they call something consistent. The full session, the full energy, the earbuds working, the favorite machine available, the body cooperating. Miss any one of those conditions and the inner critic has a ready argument: this doesn't count, try again tomorrow when things are better.

The inner critic is wrong about a lot of things. It is especially wrong about this.

A reduced-weight leg press that happens is worth more than a full-weight session that didn't. A 20-minute workout when the schedule only allowed 20 minutes is worth more than the 60-minute session that never started. A church parallel holds here: sometimes it is really hard to get there, but afterward you are always glad you went. Always.

Consistency isn't about perfect sessions. It is about showing up to imperfect ones and finding a way to continue.

## How the Inner Critic Gets In

The inner critic does not only go after the motivation. It goes after the environment.

Forgotten earbuds. A missing piece of workout clothing. The machine you wanted is already occupied. A pair of socks. These things happen. Early in the development of my practice, these events had more power over me than they deserved.

The adaptation reflex is the answer, and it gets stronger with use. Another exercise that targets the same muscle group. An indoor cycling workout instead of the treadmill. The moment the adaptation happens, something interesting occurs: the inner critic goes quiet. It does not argue against someone who just found a different route. The obstacle was supposed to end the session. Instead, a quick adaptation solved it in real time.

Sometimes the body itself provides the signal: a sluggish morning, a higher resting heart rate, or data from your tracking tools suggesting a more measured approach. The practice reads that signal and adjusts. Protect the recovery, ease off the intensity, show up differently. The inner critic would use that same signal as a reason to skip entirely. The practice uses it as information.

Snyder's Hope Theory names this precisely: hopeful people don't abandon the goal when the obvious route closes. They find another route. The inner critic can block a machine. It cannot block the next idea.

## What the Failure Mode Taught Me

Before February 2025, the inner critic won more often than it should have.

There was a gym habit, but it was steady state cardio only, no strength training, no connection to nutrition or anything else. As it turned out later, this approach actually worked slightly against the goal. The habit looked consistent but was missing key elements.

The susceptibility to derailment was high. A software upgrade blocking the workout meant a trip to the hot tub and an early exit. Comfort over growth. The TV in the early morning, the equivalent of doomscrolling, keeping the body on the couch past the point of return.

Knowing this was a failure mode led to a deliberate design decision in February 2025: the only thing that mattered was getting there with the whole self. Momentum would do the rest. That shift significantly lowered the volume of the inner critic. It still found openings occasionally: a dead earbud here, a missing piece of kit there. The baseline was different. The design had changed.

## The Season That Could Have Ended It

From the end of July through December 2025, consistency faced its real test.

Mom's health was declining. Trips to hospitals, to assisted living, the emotional and logistical weight of watching a parent near the end of her life, while carrying significant responsibility for coordination with the extended family. Many days started already exhausted, the job running in parallel with everything else.

It would have been easy to stop. It would have been completely justifiable. Nobody would have blamed the decision. The adversity was real.

Choosing to keep going meant one thing: get there.

The discovery changed everything: the practice added energy rather than depleting it. Showing up for the practice during the hardest season made showing up for Mom possible. The Renewal Loop was running in real time: contribution fueling recovery, recovery fueling contribution. The gym sessions throughout that season were not separate from the caregiving. They were what made the caregiving sustainable, allowing hope to enter the room.

## The Day Consistency Showed Up in a Hospital Room

October 2025. Mom woke up in serious pain that morning. It wasn't the first time, and the pattern had been hard to break. I was the only sibling who could go that day.

Fortunately, the morning workout was already done.

Later that day in the hospital room, a nurse practitioner spent real time with the family. For the first time in dozens of medical encounters, she raised the question of hospice care. She carefully laid out the comparison: try to resolve the cause of the pain, or focus on managing it.

Mom turned and asked me what I thought. The reply came out objective and calm. She relaxed visibly. Hospice would soon begin.

I was able to be fully present in that hospital room that day because I put myself first and prepared to face that moment without my own emotions consuming me.

The person who could be present for that conversation got there one boring, imperfect, sometimes reduced-weight morning at a time.

## Consistency as the Build Step

In the Hope Cycle (Build, Receive, Recognize, Release), consistency is the engine of the Build step. Not perfection. Not the triumphant session. Just showing up, adapting when obstacles arrive, and trusting that momentum will do the rest once you get there.

The practice that meets adversity when it matters most is the one that showed up on all the ordinary days when it didn't. The reduced-weight leg press. The cycling interval when the treadmill was taken. The workout during the week when everything else was asking you to stay home.

Those sessions don't look like a superpower. They are a superpower.

## Your Version of This

Your consistency practice may look nothing like this one. The gym may not be your arena. The adversity in your life may be completely different. The inner critic's favorite tactics may be different, too.

Think of the writer who sits down every morning for thirty minutes, even when nothing good comes out. Consider the parent who keeps showing up for a strained relationship with a child, adjusting the approach when the obvious one stops working. Picture someone rebuilding financial health, one decision at a time, adapting as the plan evolves. Or imagine someone navigating a serious health diagnosis who chooses to show up for whatever the day allows: a short walk, a single good meal, five minutes of stillness. Different arenas, different stakes. Same principle. The adaptation that silences the inner critic looks different for every person. The moment when presence made hope possible for someone else: a conversation, a decision, a day when someone needed you to be fully there. That moment has your name on it, and you are bringing the gift of hope.

I also recognize that not everyone has all that is needed to make my particular practice possible (the gym membership, the time, the resources, the support) in equal measure. The tools look different for different people. The practice doesn't.

## The Closing

Consistency is the superpower that doesn't look like one.

It looks like a reduced-weight leg press. It looks like cycling instead of the treadmill. It looks like getting there on the mornings when the caregiving and the job and the world were all asking you to stay home.

The inner critic argues with everything, except the person who keeps walking through the door.

Show up imperfect. Show up anyway.
