# A Sunset Doesn't Have Winners and Losers

### Our chaotic world is the thief of hope. Beauty reclaims it.

---

Last week at choir rehearsal, our director mixed everything up.

The rule was simple and slightly unsettling: you could not stand next to anyone from your own section. Tenors beside altos. Basses beside sopranos. The familiar crutch of leaning on the voice beside you — gone.

The song was a cappella. No piano, no accompaniment, no safety net. Just 80 people standing in unfamiliar territory, trying to make something together.

What happened next surprised me. Without the familiar crutch, everyone listened more carefully. We blended more intentionally. We focused on the voices on either side rather than defaulting to what we already knew. The group maintained pitch. The sound that emerged was something none of us could have made by leaning on what felt comfortable.

It was beautiful. And somewhere in the middle of it, standing between two people I don't usually stand beside, I felt something release. A break from the pressures of the day. A moment where the news cycle and the inner critic and the weight of everything else simply didn't have room.

I think that was hope. Not the same as hope happening when self-discipline meets adversity and wins. Something quieter. Hope received through beauty, arriving without announcing itself.

## Beauty Is Already There

Here is the thing I have come to understand about beauty: it doesn't need to be manufactured or sought dramatically. It arrives when you slow down enough to let it hit you.

Sometimes when I recognize the need to slow things down, I walk about 1.4 miles around our little lake. The ecosystem going on in that space is remarkable. Bald eagles scanning the water for fish. Muskrats cutting across the surface. Ducks and geese making an unreasonable amount of noise. The breeze moving through the grasses and the trees. Songbirds doing their work. We even have a resident albino squirrel named Reggie, who makes an appearance on occasion, entirely on his own schedule.

None of that requires my participation. It runs without me. What it requires is that I slow down enough to notice it.

Once a year I go on a fishing trip with friends. The last few years we have gone into Canada — genuinely off the grid. I am not an accomplished fisherman. I go on this trip for the friendship, for the removal from the world and from work, and for a specific moment that happens when the boat goes still. That is what quiet really sounds like. Occasionally interrupted by a fish on the line or a bird overhead, but otherwise just — quiet. That stillness is its own kind of recovery.

Music is playing almost every time I drive. Every once in a while, a track arrives and does something unexpected. Something shifts in the car before I have had a chance to decide how to feel about it. There may even be some singing that no one in the vicinity would want to hear.

Beauty in all three of these forms — music, nature, community making something together — is already present and available. The practice is the slowing down.

## What's Actually Happening in the Brain

The body knows this before the mind catches up. The activation you feel when beauty arrives is real — and the science confirms it.

Mona Lisa Chanda and Daniel Levitin synthesized research from 400 scientific papers for their 2013 review in *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*. Their finding: music reduces stress and improves mood, immunity, and social bonding through a cocktail of brain chemicals including dopamine, oxytocin, and serotonin.

Dopamine is the one most people know — the brain's reward chemical, released during moments of peak emotional intensity. The track that arrives unexpectedly in the car and does something to you before you are ready. That surge is dopamine doing its work.

Oxytocin is the one worth paying attention to in the choir. Sometimes called the social bonding hormone, oxytocin is released in community — in shared experience, in trust, in the kind of cooperation that 80 voices blending together requires. The mixing exercise our director ran last week wasn't just musical. It was biological. Eighty people leaning into each other, listening carefully, producing something none of them could produce alone — that is an oxytocin moment.

Nature adds endorphins and serotonin. The morning walk. The still boat. Movement and exposure to the natural world calming the system in ways that the news cycle never will.

The body was right. The activation is chemistry. Music, nature, and community deliver beauty to a brain in need.

## Beauty as the Receive Step

In the Hope Cycle — Build, Receive, Recognize, Release — beauty is one of the channels through which hope arrives. Not built through effort. Not earned through discipline. Received through presence.

The same mindfulness that quiets the inner critic opens the window to beauty. The curiosity that asks "why is he so noisy right now?" is the same curiosity that notices the albino squirrel or the bald eagle or the track that arrives and does something unexpected. You can walk past a sunset the same way you can walk past any other form of received hope — if you're not present enough to let it land.

Intentionality here is not about hunting for beauty. It is about choosing to slow down. The lake is there every morning. The music is already playing. The question is whether you arrive present enough to receive what the world is already offering.

## Beauty as Recovery

The choir rehearsal was a break from the pressures of the day. The fishing trip is explicitly recovery — off the grid, genuinely away. The lake walk resets something the news cycle depleted. Beauty doesn't just feel good. It does the biological work of recovery, restoring what the finite game extracts.

Simon Sinek, in *The Infinite Game*, argues that health, relationships, and meaningful work are infinite games with no finish line and no final winner. The news cycle is a finite game — winners and losers, urgency and score-keeping, extracting from the practice without replenishing it. Beauty never signed up for the finite contest. It just keeps going.

Dr. Gervais says there is no off-season, only seasons of emphasis. Beauty is one of the mechanisms through which the practice refuels itself between the harder seasons. Not the grind kind of hope. The receiving kind. The lake in the morning. The still boat. The choir finding its pitch together.

The Renewal Loop runs through beauty too. Contribution fuels recovery, and recovery fuels contribution. Receiving beauty — genuinely, intentionally, with presence — gives the practice something to draw on.

## The Closing

The beauty is already there. The practice is already running.

The lake will be there tomorrow. Music will be in the car. The choir will gather again. The boat will go still sometime this summer.

The only question is whether you slow down enough to let it arrive. Receive what the world is offering. That is the Hope Cycle completing itself, quietly, in the spaces between the hard work.
