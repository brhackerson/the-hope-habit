## Core Idea

Great leaders don't just carry hope — they spread it. Hope leadership is not performing optimism or saying "everything will be fine." It is being honest about the adversity and still pointing toward a path. That's a harder and more interesting thing to do.

Key distinction: inspiring hope vs. false comfort.

## Research Anchors

**Snyder's Hope Theory — leadership angle**
High-hope leaders build agency and pathways in the people around them. The leader's role is not to be the only hopeful person in the room — it's to create conditions where others can find their own agency and pathways.
- Source: to be developed further

**Fewell, Jesse. *Untapped Agility*. Berrett-Koehler Publishers, 2020. Chapter 7: "Look in the Mirror" (p. 123)**
- Brian is the subject of Chapter 7
- The story: Brian's transformation work at 3M scaled to 1,000 people. The imposter took up residence. Mindfulness practice (via Dr. Michael Gervais) created the reflective capacity to see what was happening. The inflection point: "Color came back into my life."
- The hope leadership connection: Brian's capacity to lead returned the moment he could see agency and pathways again. That is the hope practice in action inside a leadership story.
- Key Fewell principle: "An organization's level of agility cannot exceed the level of agility of its leaders." Substitute hope for agility — the same principle holds.
- Joiner's Reflective Capacity cycle (assess, diagnose, set intentions, act, reassess) — documented in this chapter. Relevant to the ABS retrospective angle.

## ABS Connection

Principle 1: Our highest priority is to be our best self and enable others to be their best selves. Hope leadership is that principle in action. Being your best self and enabling others are not separate acts — they are the same act.
