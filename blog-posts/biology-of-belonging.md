# The Biology of Belonging

### Community isn't a nice-to-have for your hope practice. The science says it's essential.

In Post 1 of this series, I described two rooms — a small church gathering and a concert arena — and what they had in common. Both were communities pointed in the same direction. Both held hope in a way that no individual could hold alone.

This post goes one level deeper. Not what community is, but what it actually does. Why is the hope practice harder to sustain on one's own than within a community? And why that isn't a personal failing — it's biology.

## We Are Social Animals First

In his book, *The First Rule of Mastery*, Dr. Michael Gervais makes an argument that reframes the whole conversation. He writes on page 151: "We are not individuals who have learned to be social. We are social animals who have learned to identify as separate selves."

Read that again. We didn't start as individuals and then figure out how to get along. We started as social creatures, and modern culture taught us to think of ourselves as separate. The need to belong, to connect, to be part of something larger — that isn't weakness or dependency. It is the original architecture.

Community is not an add-on to the hope practice. It is a return to what we actually are.

## What the Brain Knows

Dr. Sanjay Gupta makes the biological case in Chapter 8 of *Keep Sharp*: "Connection for Protection." Social connection isn't just good for the soul. It is as essential to brain health as exercise, sleep, and nutrition. Not a supplement — a pillar. The brain needs it to stay resilient, to build the cognitive reserve that protects against decline, and to sustain the kind of purposeful, engaged thinking that makes a hope practice possible.

Community is where that connection lives. Not the scroll through a feed. Not a text exchange. Real connection — shared experience, shared values, shared direction — in the rooms where belonging actually happens.

## What the Research on Hope Confirms

Psychologist C. R. Snyder spent decades establishing Hope Theory — the two engines of hope are **agency**, the belief you can move forward, and **pathways**, the ability to find a route when the obvious one is blocked. Neither is passive. Both require action.

Snyder's original framework focused on the individual. But in 2022, researchers Colla, Williams, Oades, and Camacho-Morles published an extension of his work in *Frontiers in Psychology*, adding two new dimensions. The first dimension they called Why Power — the meaning dimension, the sense that your goals are connected to something larger than yourself. The second dimension they called We Power — the interpersonal dimension, the recognition that hope doesn't just emerge from within the individual. Social resources, shared connections, and the dynamic interplay between people and their communities fuel its growth.

The research confirmed what the practice already knew. Hope is not purely a solo pursuit. It is built and sustained in relationship with others.

## A Practical Argument

James Clear makes a practical argument in *Atomic Habits* that connects directly to this. Build better habits, he writes, by joining a culture where your desired behavior is the normal behavior — where you already have something in common with the group.

Community normalizes the hope practice. When the people around you are also building something, showing up consistently, choosing the long game over the easy morning — the practice becomes what people like you do. You don't have to fight the current. You swim with it.

## Drawing From Multiple Communities

Most people already belong to multiple communities without naming it that way. A faith community. A professional community. A like-hearted online community. A close circle of friends. Each one feeds something different. Together, they create a resilience that no single community could provide alone.

Your contribution to each community multiplies rather than divides. It gets amplified. One person showing up consistently in multiple communities means the ripple travels in multiple directions at once.

You don't have to pick one. You don't have to commit to a formal structure. All that is needed is to show up, share what you have, and receive what the community offers in return.

## Second-Level: The Impact You Don't See

Here is the part that requires the most trust.

You don't always get to see what your contribution does. You don't see who reads a post at 3 am. You don't see whose practice shifts because of something you said. You don't see how your presence in someone's life changed who they were for the people in their life.

That's second-level impact. You don't just change the person in front of you. You change who they are for the people in front of them. The hope you release into a community doesn't need a receipt. It just needs to be released.

The research calls it We Power. The practice calls it showing up. Both are pointing at the same thing.

## The Invitation

When we built the Agile Best Self community and mindset, Michaele came up with the idea of a like-hearted community rather than a like-minded one. It sounded warmer and more inviting, a feeling our community of Agile change agents craved at that time.

Agile Best Self Principle 11 states it directly: the best inspirations and insights emerge from like-hearted communities. Not the loudest community. Not the largest. The one that shares your values and points in the same direction.

If you don't have that community yet, look for it. If it doesn't exist, consider building it. It doesn't have to be large. It doesn't have to be formal. It just has to be real.

Two people pointing in the same direction are a community. One person creating space for others to gather is a community in the making.

The biology was always on your side. The science provides you with the receipt.
