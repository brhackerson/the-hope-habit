# Two Rooms

### It's not the size of the room. It's what the room is pointed toward.

February 2026 was a heavy month.

The news in the Twin Cities was hard to sit with, and my mom had passed away only weeks earlier. I was still carrying that grief, not loudly but constantly, the way grief settles into the background of everything you do.

My son suggested we go to a meeting at our church. The congregation was talking about how we could respond to what was happening in our community: neighbors being targeted and hunted, people afraid, a city trying to figure out how to show up for each other. I was hesitant. I am not inclined to publicly protest, as I am very aware of the personal risk of being in the wrong place at the wrong time. I was curious whether or not there was any way I could help, so I agreed to go with my son.

## The First Room

Fewer than 20 people gathered that night. Familiar faces, people I had seen across years of Sunday mornings, some whose names I didn't already know, but whose presence I recognized. We weren't strangers, and we weren't close friends. We were something in between: a congregation, people who had been in the same room many times without fully knowing each other.

There was no grand agenda and no speeches. People shared quiet stories about what they had done: packing food at a food shelf, showing up at an event, writing a letter. Small acts, personal acts, done not because anyone told them to, but because something inside them said this is what I can do. The takeaway I left with was simple: it doesn't matter what you do. Do what is in your heart.

My star word HOPE had arrived in a communion line only weeks earlier, a week after my mom passed, and I hadn't fully known what to do with it yet. Sitting in that room, still heavy with grief, listening to familiar faces talk about quiet acts done from conviction, something shifted. What if acting on HOPE meant writing? What if exploring my hope practice publicly could help someone who was struggling to feel hopeful in the face of real adversity, on the ground, in our cities, right now?

That question started everything. The Substack. The posts. This work. All of it traces back to a room of fewer than 20 familiar faces on a cold February night.

## The Second Room

Five weeks later, I was in a very different room.

Target Center in Minneapolis, Bruce Springsteen and the E Street Band, thousands of people. Springsteen closed the night with something I wasn't expecting. He talked about hope, about community, about the distance between neighbors that darkens the soul. About common humanity, dignity, and unity. He invoked John Lewis: "Get into good trouble. Say something. Do something. Help." And then he said something that has stayed with me in the days that followed:

*"This is a tour that was not planned. We're here tonight because we need to feel your hope and your strength. And we want to bring some hope and some strength for you."*

A room of thousands, pointing in the same direction. An artist saying to his community, the state of Minnesota: I need what you have, and I want to give you what I have. That exchange, that mutual offering, is what community does at its best — different scale from the church room, and the same truth.

## What Community Actually Is

I've been thinking about both rooms ever since, and what strikes me most is how little the size mattered.

Community is not a large group or a formal organization. It is not people who all know each other deeply. Community is shared values coming together to create experiences meant to uplift all. You don't need history with someone to be in community with them. You need alignment, direction, and a shared sense of what matters. You don't need to know someone's name for community to work. All you need to do is move in the same direction.

I see community as different from the trusted circle, the small group of people who know your real story, hold your specific hope when yours runs out, and show up when things are hardest. The trusted circle is intimate and personal. Community is something broader in kind. It reminds you why hope matters in the first place and holds the larger purpose that makes the daily practice worth sustaining. You need both, and neither replaces the other.

## What These Two Rooms Taught Me

The room at church taught me that a small community practicing hope quietly, without fanfare or audience, can change the direction of someone's life. It had that effect on mine. The arena taught me that community scales, that hope offered across a room of thousands is the same hope offered across a room of 20, and that the exchange works at any size.

Both rooms reminded me of something I keep coming back to: you don't need a large community to feel its power. You need a real one. Even a room of familiar faces pointing in the same direction is enough.

Look for it. Show up for it. Let it remind you why your hope practice matters.
