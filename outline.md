## Introduction: The Word I Didn't Know I Needed

The story of Epiphany Sunday 2025. A word arrives — hope — and it feels like a gift from a mother who is leaving. A doctor's visit that makes things very clear. A 60th birthday approaching. The decision that becomes a journey. And the discovery that changes everything: hope is not a feeling. It's a practice worth building.

This book is not a prescription. It is a personal framework and a generous invitation. Here is what I learned. Here are the principles underneath it. Now go find what works for you.

The missing first step — before the practice, before the self-discipline, there is a moment of choice. Hope is a decision. The decision precedes everything. Without it, the practice never starts. This insight, captured from a quiet moment at an NDSU choir concert, may be the most important sentence in the book.

The Agile and Scrum mindset as foundational context — inspect and adapt, empiricism, the sprint retrospective — not the subject of the book but the scaffolding that made building a hope practice possible. You don't build a hope practice from scratch. You build it from where you are.

*Key sources:* Sutherland (Scrum values), Agile Best Self principles

## Part One: Why Hope Is Not What You Think

*Part One challenges the conventional understanding of hope and replaces it with something more honest, more useful, and more powerful. By the end of this section the reader understands what hope actually is, why it requires action, and what gets in the way of building it.*

### Chapter 1: The Lie We Tell Ourselves About Hope
Most of us have been waiting for hope to show up like a feeling — a mood, a disposition, a gift the optimists have in abundance and the rest of us wait for. That waiting is the lie. Hope is not a feeling. It is something you do.

Snyder's Hope Theory introduced — agency and pathways as the two engines of real hope. Both require action. Neither is passive. Hope is cognitive, not emotional — a motivational state directed at specific goals through specific routes.

How is this different from grit? Duckworth's grit is about staying the course. Hope is about finding the course, losing it, and finding it again. Grit says keep going. Hope says keep looking for the way. You need both — but they are not the same thing.

How is this different from optimism? Optimism is a disposition — a general belief that things will work out. Hope is specific, active, and directed. Optimism prepares the soil. Hope does the planting.

Manson's counterintuitive framing — *Everything is F*cked* argues that the loss of hope in modern life comes from our inability to tolerate discomfort and our confusion about what is worth hoping for. A hope practice requires radical honesty about reality — not positive thinking, not wishful optimism, but clear-eyed action toward something that genuinely matters.

*Story woven in:* The years of knowing what to do and not doing it. The information. The intention. The failed attempts. The inner critic winning too many mornings in a row.

*Key sources:* Snyder, Duckworth (Grit), Manson (Everything is F*cked)

### Chapter 2: What Hope Actually Is
A deeper exploration of Snyder's framework. Agency — the belief that you can move forward, the goal-directed energy that gets you out of bed at 5am. Pathways — the ability to find or create a route when the obvious one is blocked, the willingness to adjust when something isn't working. Hopeful people are more clear-eyed about obstacles than pure optimists — they are already looking for alternative routes.

Pink's three elements of intrinsic motivation — autonomy, mastery, purpose — as a complementary lens. Hope needs all three. You need to own your practice (autonomy). You need to be moving toward something meaningful (purpose). You need to be getting better at it over time (mastery).

Sinek's infinite game reframed through hope — you're not playing to finish, you're playing to keep playing. Hope is the fuel for an infinite game worth playing. A just cause. A future worth sacrificing for. An identity worth becoming.

*Story woven in:* The bad doctor's visit. The moment things became clear. The decision to give myself a gift — to myself and my family.

*Key sources:* Snyder, Pink (Drive), Sinek (Infinite Game)

### Chapter 3: The Neuroscience of Hope
The biological case for building a hope practice. What happens in the brain when we act with agency and purpose. Exercise, sleep, nutrition, and social connection as the physical infrastructure of hope — not optional lifestyle choices but the foundational conditions that make sustained hope possible.

Gupta's Keep Sharp as the science behind the journey — the brain is not fixed, it is plastic, and the choices you make every day either strengthen or weaken it. The health transformation and the hope practice were not separate things. They were the same thing, reinforcing each other at a biological level.

The surprising truth — building a hope practice isn't just good for your soul. It's good for your brain. And a better brain builds better hope.

*Story woven in:* The early morning workouts. The intentional nutrition. The sleep discipline. Feeling sharper, clearer, more capable as the practice deepened. The post-surgery recovery as further evidence — the body and brain respond to consistent, intentional care.

*Key sources:* Gupta (Keep Sharp)

### Chapter 4: The Foundation You're Already Standing On
You don't build a hope practice from scratch. You build it from where you are — from the values you already hold, the frameworks you've already internalized, the work you've already done on yourself.

The Scrum values — commitment, focus, openness, respect, courage — as the bedrock of how Brian approaches everything. These weren't adopted for this journey. They were already there, waiting to be applied to the most personal project of his life.

The historical context matters: the Scrum Values were not part of Scrum from the beginning. Dr. Sutherland and Schwaber added them in the 2016 revision of the Scrum Guide — because the mechanics of Scrum alone were not enough. The human dimension had to be named explicitly. Hope practice works the same way. The habits, the keyword, the daily routine are the mechanics. The values are what make the practice sustainable at a human level. Each value maps directly to a dimension of the hope practice: courage to meet adversity, commitment as self-discipline in action, focus as the keyword doing its work, openness as Principle 12 (inspect and adjust), and respect starting with yourself — the foundation a hope practice collapses without.

### Chapter 5: Silencing What Holds You Back
The inner critic is the primary obstacle to a hope practice. It is the voice that tells you the alarm doesn't matter, that you've tried before and failed, that you're not the kind of person who does this. It is lying.

Gervais's FOPO — Fear of Other People's Opinions — as a specific and powerful form of the inner critic. The worry about what others think quietly undermines agency and pathways before you even get started.

Brown's Daring Greatly — vulnerability is not weakness. It is the courage to show up and be seen, imperfect and in progress. Shame is what the inner critic weaponizes — and shame resilience is what the inner advocate builds in response. Writing publicly about a health journey. Admitting you're not where you want to be. All of these require daring greatly.

Robbins's 5 Second Rule as a practical tool — when the inner critic speaks, you count. 5-4-3-2-1. And then you move. The hesitation window is where the critic lives. Close it before it opens.

Hope requires courage. The Scrum value of courage shows up here — the willingness to do the right thing even when it's hard, to show up when showing up is the last thing you want to do.

*Story woven in:* The 5am alarm. The inner critic winning too many mornings. The 5 Second Rule in the weight room. The moment the inner advocate started winning instead. Writing the blog posts — daring greatly in public.

*Key sources:* Gervais (First Rule of Mastery), Brown (Daring Greatly), Robbins (5 Second Rule), Sutherland (Scrum values)

## Part Two: Building Your Hope Practice

*Part Two is the heart of the book — the practical framework for building a hope practice that is uniquely yours. It is organized around three questions: What are you hoping toward? How will you build and sustain the practice? What will you protect it from? Daniel Pink's autonomy, mastery, and purpose provide the structural spine.*

### Chapter 6: Purpose — Find Your Why and Make It a Keyword
Sinek's Start With Why as the foundation — people don't sustain what they do, they sustain why they do it. Purpose is not decoration. It is the engine.

Gervais's three criteria for a personal philosophy as the framework for the keyword mechanism — a word or phrase that is 25 words or less (shorter is better), speakable under duress, and felt somewhere in the body when spoken with others. These criteria explain why the keyword works, not just that it works. The physical sensation is neuroscience — Gupta's Keep Sharp in action.

The keyword mechanism as purpose made operational — a single word that in your hardest moment reconnects you to your deepest why in seconds. Not a motivational poster. Not a complicated system. One word, accessed instantly, at 5am when every part of you wants to stay in bed.

How "Pictures" became the reset trigger — and how it passes all three of Gervais's criteria exactly. One word. Speakable at 5am or mid-rep. And viscerally felt when you talk about what it means. The legacy frame — what story will your presence tell across generations? The photographs that will outlive you. What will your kids tell their grandchildren when they look at those pictures?

Manson's values framework from *The Subtle Art* — what are you willing to suffer for? The keyword is your answer to that question made concrete and portable. It must be worth the suffering. It must survive your worst moments.

How to find your own keyword. What makes a keyword work. Why specificity matters more than inspiration. How to test it against Gervais's three criteria.

*Story woven in:* The keyword "Pictures." 5am alarms. The moments the word saved the morning. The future photographs already worth living for — graduations, weddings, grandchildren.

*Key sources:* Sinek (Start With Why), Gervais (First Rule of Mastery — personal philosophy criteria, course), Manson (Subtle Art), Pink (Drive — purpose), Snyder, Gupta (Keep Sharp)

### Chapter 7: Autonomy — The System Nobody Else Can Build For You
Pink's autonomy principle — the hope practice must be yours, not someone else's prescription. No formula. No program. No one else's system transplanted into your life.

The iterative approach — Sutherland's inspect and adapt cycle applied to personal transformation. Running experiments on yourself. Paying attention to what the data is actually telling you, especially when it surprises you. The post-practice review after every workout — how did that feel? Could I come back tomorrow?

Clear's identity-based habits — you're not just building a hope practice, you're becoming a hopeful person. The practice changes the identity. The identity sustains the practice.

Harris's mindfulness framework — the capacity to observe your own system without judgment is a prerequisite for hacking it. Meditation as a tool for building the self-awareness that makes personal experimentation possible.

*Story woven in:* The year as a series of experiments. What worked. What didn't. What each failure taught. The shift from following someone else's formula to trusting your own data.

*Key sources:* Pink (Drive — autonomy), Clear (Atomic Habits), Sutherland (inspect and adapt), Harris (Meditation for Fidgety Skeptics)

### Chapter 8: Mastery — Measuring What Actually Matters
Pink's mastery principle — progress toward something that matters, measured in ways that actually reflect what's happening. The wrong metric and what it costs you.

The scale as a false signal. The plateau that sparked curiosity. The shift from weight to lean muscle mass as the real measure of progress. When the right metric replaced the wrong one, progress that had been invisible became visible.

Snyder's Hope Scale as a self-assessment tool — measuring hope itself, not just its outcomes. Clear's habit tracking — what gets measured gets managed, but only if you're measuring the right thing.

*Story woven in:* The plateau. The curiosity. The shift that changed everything. The dumbbell photos as a different kind of measurement — not numbers but evidence.

*Key sources:* Pink (Drive — mastery), Snyder (Hope Scale), Clear (Atomic Habits — tracking)

### Chapter 9: You Can't Build It Alone
The trusted circle — not formal accountability but the right people with the right expertise at the right time. Brown's BRAVING inventory as a framework for understanding what trust actually requires — boundaries, reliability, accountability, vault, integrity, non-judgment, generosity.

Pink's relatedness — connection is not separate from the hope practice. It is infrastructure. The doctor. The gym community — loose but real. A wife who believed without controlling, pursuing her own parallel journey without making Brian's journey hers to manage.

Dare to Lead's insight — leading yourself requires the same courage as leading others. The trusted circle is not just support. It is the community of people with whom you dare greatly.

*Story woven in:* Building the team. The gym as community. A wife who believed without controlling. The doctor as expert partner.

*Key sources:* Brown (Dare to Lead — BRAVING), Pink (Drive — relatedness), Agile Best Self Principle 4

### Chapter 10: The Two Sides of the Practice
Building a hope practice isn't just about what you add — it's about what you protect against. Clear's environment design and habit protection are as important as habit formation. The things that quietly erode what you're building deserve the same intentional attention as the things you're building.

Intentional reduction as protection, not deprivation — cutting alcohol, reducing sugar and refined carbs, framed not as sacrifice but as acts of loyalty to the practice.

Recovery as part of the design — sleep, rest days, intentional rewards woven close to the behavior. The hot tub. The new workout shorts. Small things that signal to your system that the work is valued and the worker matters.

Manson's values clarity — what are you willing to protect? Agile Best Self Principle 8 — prioritize being more over doing more. Sustainability is a principle, not an afterthought.

*Story woven in:* The intentional reductions. The hot tub. The new workout shorts. Learning what Principle 8 means in practice. The post-surgery recovery as protecting the practice through disruption.

*Key sources:* Clear (Atomic Habits — environment design), Manson (Subtle Art — values), Agile Best Self Principle 8

### Chapter 11: The Ritual That Found Me
You don't always design your most powerful practices. Sometimes they find you.

The dumbbell milestone ritual — picking up each weight corresponding to lbs lost, feeling in your hands what your body had been carrying every single moment of every single day. No breaks. No putting it down. Just constant load.

Sutherland's retrospective reframed — most retros lose power over time as novelty fades. This one got more powerful as the numbers climbed, because the stakes kept rising and the evidence kept accumulating. Hope compounding on itself.

The double meaning of the weight you were carrying — not just physical weight but the doctor's visit, the years of failed attempts, the responsibility to your family, the legacy question.

Clear's concept of making progress visible — the photos as evidence, proof, a tangible record that the identity is changing.

*Story woven in:* The full dumbbell journey — 10 through 85 lbs. The Life Time gym. The photos. The intensification. The 85 lb dumbbell you can barely lift with one arm.

*Key sources:* Sutherland (retrospective), Clear (Atomic Habits — progress visibility), Agile Best Self Principle 12

## Part Three: What the Practice Makes Possible

*Part Three looks forward. The hope practice is not a means to an end — it is the thing itself, sustained and deepened over time. This section shows what becomes possible when hope is no longer something you wait for but something you have built.*

### Chapter 12: Hope Has a Target
Hope is not vague aspiration. It needs something specific to aim at — a future moment you are actively moving toward, not just wishing for.

Sinek's just cause — something worth sacrificing for, something larger than yourself, something that pulls you forward even on the hardest days. The Pictures concept as a just cause made personal and specific. Graduations. Weddings. Grandchildren. Breaking 80. Walking 18 holes. Arriving at retirement in the best shape of your recent life.

Conley's Modern Elder framing — the second half of life is not a slow decline. It is a season of purpose and contribution built on a foundation of wisdom that younger people simply don't have yet. The hope practice at 60 aims at a different kind of target than it would at 30 — deeper, richer, more specific, more earned.

Pink's purpose at its fullest expression — the infinite game version of a life well lived. Not a finish line but a horizon that keeps expanding as you move toward it.

*Story woven in:* The golf journey — 50 years, competitive roots, rededication to the game, the goal of breaking 80 consistently in retirement. Walking the course. The Orlando trip. The ride at the theme park with the harness that will finally close.

*Key sources:* Snyder, Sinek (Infinite Game), Pink (Drive — purpose), Conley (Wisdom at Work)

### Chapter 13: Hope Forward
The most powerful hope is the hope that reaches beyond your own lifetime.

Legacy as a hope practice. What the photographs will say long after you are gone. The father lost to Alzheimer's after a decade of slow departure. The mother who gave you the word on Epiphany Sunday and then left in December. What it means to be the oldest of four children — the family steward, the keeper of the story.

Hope expressed outward becomes kindness. The mother's kindness practice — knitting stuffed animals for charity, the handmade embroidered dish towels as a quiet currency of care, the monkey found in an ambulance ("I made that") — is what hope looks like when it turns outward toward others. This thread runs through the book and finds its fullest expression here: the hope practice you build for yourself eventually becomes something you give away.

Hope expressed outward becomes kindness as practice — the thread that honors Brian's mother directly. The knitted stuffed animals donated to charity. The monkey found in an ambulance. The handmade embroidered dish towels as kindness currency — "no dish towel for you." These were not random acts of generosity. They were a hope practice turned outward, a daily discipline of making the world a little warmer for strangers. This is what hope looks like when it reaches beyond the self and into the lives of others.

Brown's wholehearted living — engaging with life from a place of worthiness, daring greatly not just for yourself but for the people who will come after you. Vulnerability in service of legacy.

Sinek's infinite game at its deepest level — a just cause that outlives the player. The hope practice you build today is a gift to people who haven't been born yet. That is not vanity. That is legacy.

*Story woven in:* The passing of both parents. The weight of stewardship. The gift of the word "hope" from a mother who was leaving. The photographs that will tell the story across generations.

*Key sources:* Sinek (Infinite Game — just cause), Brown (Daring Greatly — wholehearted living)

### Chapter 14: The Practice Never Ends
Hope practices mature. They evolve. The early milestones come quickly and the retrospectives are urgent and emotional. Then the milestones space out, the wins get quieter, and the foundation gets deeper. This is not regression. This is maturity.

Duckworth's long game — grit and hope are companions here. The practice requires the willingness to keep showing up long after the novelty has faded, long after the dramatic early wins, long after anyone is watching. But where Duckworth asks "do you have what it takes?" the hope practice asks "what does your practice need right now?"

Agile Best Self Principle 12 — at regular intervals, reflect on how to become your best self, then tune and adjust. The practice is never finished. It is always being refined. That is not a flaw. That is the design.

The post-surgery recovery as a case study — a disruption that could have broken the practice instead became a demonstration of how resilient a well-built hope practice can be. Back on the treadmill by Day 2. Running by Day 13. The framework held.

Conley's wisdom — with age comes the capacity to hold complexity, to sustain effort without the adrenaline of novelty, to find meaning in the quiet seasons as much as the dramatic ones. The hope practice at its most mature is not loud. It is deep.

*Story woven in:* The hernia surgery and recovery. The milestones spacing out. The practice becoming quieter and more sustainable. Golf as the ongoing horizon. The next chapter already beginning.

*Key sources:* Duckworth (Grit — long game), Agile Best Self Principle 12, Conley (Wisdom at Work)

## Conclusion: Build Yours

The invitation. This is not a prescription — it is a personal framework and a generous offer. Here is what I learned. Here are the principles underneath it. Now go find what works for you.

Your keyword. Your metric. Your circle. Your ritual. Your pictures.

You are already standing on a foundation. The reading you've done. The work you've already put in on yourself. The values you already hold. The people already in your corner. None of that is nothing. All of it is somewhere to start.

Manson's final question repurposed — what are you willing to hope for? Not what you wish would happen. What you are willing to act toward, protect, sustain, and return to on your hardest mornings.

Sinek's closing note — the infinite game is worth playing. Not because you will win. Because the playing itself is the point. The practice itself is the hope.

Hope is not a feeling. It's a practice worth building. And it starts wherever you are.
